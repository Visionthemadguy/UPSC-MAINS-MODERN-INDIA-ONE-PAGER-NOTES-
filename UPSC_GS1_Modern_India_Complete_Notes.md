# MICROTHEME 1
## Establishment and Expansion of British Power in India (mid-18th to mid-19th century)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The transformation of the East India Company from a trading entity to a sovereign territorial power is one of the most consequential developments in world history. Between 1757 and 1857, a commercial corporation systematically dismantled established Indian polities through a combination of military force, political manipulation, and diplomatic cunning, laying the foundations of over a century of colonial rule.

**Introduction 2 (Conceptual):** British expansion in India was not a single event but a process — driven by commercial interests, European power rivalries, the political fragmentation of post-Mughal India, and a calculated imperial strategy. Understanding this process is essential for comprehending every subsequent dimension of India's colonial experience.

**Introduction 3 (Policy-based):** Three instruments — the Subsidiary Alliance, the Doctrine of Lapse, and the direct use of military force — formed the triangular architecture of British territorial expansion across the Indian subcontinent between 1798 and 1856.

**Introduction 4 (Analytical):** The Battle of Plassey in 1757 was not merely a military victory; it was a political revolution. It converted the Company from a supplicant trading body into the de facto sovereign of Bengal — and the revenues of Bengal then financed the conquest of the rest of India.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Historical Background: India at the Mid-18th Century
The mid-18th century saw India in a state of political fragmentation following the decline of the Mughal Empire after Aurangzeb's death (1707). Regional powers — the Marathas, Nizams, Nawabs of Bengal, Awadh, and Mysore — competed for supremacy. This power vacuum created an opportunity that the European trading companies, especially the English East India Company (EIC, founded 1600), exploited with remarkable effectiveness. The EIC had initially been content to trade from coastal enclaves (Surat, Madras, Bombay, Calcutta). The European rivalry — particularly Anglo-French competition during the Carnatic Wars (1740s–1760s) — pushed the Company toward military engagement. The French had demonstrated that political dominance, not just trade, determined commercial success. This lesson was decisive.

#### 2. The Decisive Turn: Plassey to Buxar
The **Battle of Plassey (June 23, 1757)** was the foundational moment. Robert Clive defeated Nawab Sirajuddaulah of Bengal not through military superiority alone but through conspiracy — Mir Jafar, the Nawab's general, was bribed to betray him. The aftermath installed Mir Jafar as a puppet Nawab, making the Company the real power behind Bengal's throne. This model of "kingmaker colonialism" became the template.

The **Battle of Buxar (October 22, 1764)** was strategically more significant than Plassey. The Company defeated a combined force of Nawab Mir Qasim (Bengal), Nawab Shuja-ud-Daula (Awadh), and the Mughal Emperor Shah Alam II. This established British military supremacy not just over Bengal but over the Mughal-Nawab coalition — demonstrating the Company could defeat even a constitutionally legitimized Mughal emperor.

The **Grant of Diwani (1765)** was the constitutionally pivotal moment. The Mughal Emperor granted the Company revenue collection rights (Diwani) over Bengal, Bihar, and Orissa. This converted the Company from a foreign merchant into a de facto feudal lord within the Mughal constitutional framework, while the Nawab retained Nizamat (administrative and judicial) power — creating the "Dual Government" that Warren Hastings later abolished (1772). The Diwani gave the Company an assured revenue base to fund further military expansion — effectively using Bengal's wealth to conquer the rest of India.

#### 3. Governors-General and Their Expansion Strategies
British expansion accelerated under a series of ambitious Governors-General, each contributing distinct methods:

**Warren Hastings (1772–1785):** First Governor-General under the Regulating Act (1773), which attempted parliamentary oversight of the Company. Hastings abolished Dual Government, centralised administration, and fought the First Anglo-Maratha War (1775–82) and Second Anglo-Mysore War (1780–84). His impeachment trial (led by Edmund Burke) revealed the moral contradictions of colonial rule even to British opinion.

**Lord Cornwallis (1786–1793):** Introduced the Permanent Settlement (1793) transforming zamindars into landlords. Established the Cornwallis Code — separating judicial and executive powers, and Indianizing only the lower rungs (excluding Indians from higher posts — creating the foundation of racial discrimination in administration). Fought the Third Anglo-Mysore War (1792), defeating Tipu Sultan and forcing him to cede half his territory.

**Lord Wellesley (1798–1805):** The architect of the **Subsidiary Alliance system**. Under this doctrine, Indian rulers received British military protection in exchange for: (a) permanent stationing of British troops in their territory; (b) paying for these troops (or ceding territory if unable); (c) excluding other European advisors; (d) conducting foreign policy only through the Company. In practice, this bankrupted Indian states and made them satellites. Hyderabad (1798), Mysore (1799 after defeating Tipu Sultan in the Fourth Anglo-Mysore War), the Maratha chiefs Scindia, Bhonsle, and Holkar were all brought within this net. The Third Anglo-Mysore War had already weakened Mysore; Wellesley's Fourth War (1799) ended it. Tipu Sultan died defending Seringapatam — becoming India's first nationalist martyr in colonial memory.

**Lord Hastings (1813–1823):** Established British paramountcy by definitively defeating the Marathas in the Third/Fourth Anglo-Maratha War (1817–18), ending the last major Indian challenge to British supremacy. The Peshwa was exiled to Bithur (and his adopted son Nana Sahib would lead the 1857 Revolt). Simultaneously crushed the Pindaris — predatory irregular forces — whose elimination removed the last destabilizing military formations outside British control.

**Lord Dalhousie (1848–1856):** The most aggressive expansionist. Used the **Doctrine of Lapse** — under which any princely state whose ruler died without a natural heir would lapse to the Company, regardless of adopted heirs. Applied to Satara (1848), Jaitpur and Sambalpur (1849), Baghat (1850), Udaipur (1852), Jhansi (1853), and Nagpur (1854). Also annexed Awadh (1856) — not through lapse but on grounds of "misgovernance" — a particularly provocative act since Awadh was the heartland of the Bengal Army's sepoy recruitment, directly igniting the 1857 Revolt. Dalhousie also introduced railways (first line 1853), telegraph (1853), and the Uniform Postage system — modernizations that were simultaneously instruments of commercial exploitation and military control.

#### 4. Key Instruments of Conquest
**Military superiority:** The Company's army, though largely composed of Indian sepoys, was disciplined, paid regularly, and equipped with superior tactics and artillery. Indian rulers often had larger forces but poorer organizational capacity and internal loyalty.

**Political manipulation:** The Company exploited succession disputes, rivalries between nobles, and the debt of Indian rulers. The Subsidiary Alliance was specifically designed to create financial dependency leading to territorial cession.

**The Regulating Act (1773)** established parliamentary oversight, appointing a Governor-General and a Supreme Court — acknowledging that the Company's conquest had public significance. **Pitt's India Act (1784)** created a Board of Control — dual governance where a government-appointed board oversaw political matters while the Company's Court of Directors handled commercial affairs. This structure persisted until 1858. **Charter Acts of 1813 and 1833** progressively eroded the Company's commercial monopoly (trade opened to all British merchants in 1813; Company's commercial functions ended in 1833, leaving it purely as an administrative body).

#### 5. Nature of British Conquest
British conquest was racially framed but institutionally ambiguous — the Company claimed to be reforming India while exploiting it. The racial dimension was clear in administrative appointments (Cornwallis Code) and in social attitudes. Historiographically, James Mill's periodization (Hindu-Muslim-British) imposed a framework that justified British conquest as historical progress — a teleological distortion challenged by modern historians like Irfan Habib, Bipan Chandra, and Romila Thapar.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** The British conquest of India, though exploitative, inadvertently created the administrative, legal, and railway infrastructure that later facilitated both nationalist mobilisation and post-independence nation-building — a paradox at the heart of colonial modernity.

**Conclusion 2 (Legacy):** The instruments of British conquest — the Subsidiary Alliance, the Doctrine of Lapse, racial administration — planted grievances that erupted in 1857 and seeded the long struggle for independence. Understanding how India was conquered is inseparable from understanding how India sought freedom.

**Conclusion 3 (Constitutional):** The progressive transformation of the East India Company from trade to sovereignty — regulated by parliamentary Acts from 1773 to 1833 — reveals the deep entanglement of British capitalism and imperialism, a connection that nationalist economists like Dadabhai Naoroji later exposed through the Drain of Wealth theory.

**Conclusion 4 (Forward-looking):** The patterns of British expansion — diplomatic manipulation, economic extraction, and military dominance — have continued relevance for understanding how powerful states interact with weaker ones, making this history not merely antiquarian but a living lens on international relations.

# MICROTHEME 2
## Colonial Revenue Systems and Agrarian Policies

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Land revenue was the financial backbone of colonial rule. The British transformed India's complex, community-based land relations into a simplified, extractive system designed primarily to generate a stable and growing revenue for the colonial state — with profound consequences for peasant life, social structure, and the rural economy.

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** Three major land revenue systems — the Permanent Settlement (1793), the Ryotwari System (from the 1820s), and the Mahalwari System (1822) — covered virtually all of British India by the mid-19th century. Each was different in design but similar in effect: systematically shifting the burden of revenue extraction onto Indian cultivators while insulating the colonial state from the consequences of agrarian distress.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** Romesh Chandra Dutt's *Economic History of India* (1902) and Dadabhai Naoroji's *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India* (1901) established the thesis that colonial revenue policy was the primary engine of Indian poverty — a thesis that later nationalist economists and post-colonial historians have substantially validated.

**Introduction 4 (Quote-based):** As Dadabhai Naoroji argued, the colonial revenue system created a situation where "India is being bled" — revenues extracted from the land were remitted to Britain as "Home Charges," constituting the core mechanism of the Drain of Wealth.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Pre-Colonial Revenue Context
Pre-colonial Indian land revenue was complex and varied. Under the Mughals, the Ain-i-Akbari system (Todar Mal's reforms under Akbar) calculated revenue based on average produce over ten years, with flexibility built in. Land was not privately owned in the Western sense; the peasant had hereditary rights of cultivation (Ryotwari), while the state claimed a share of produce. Village communities (mahalwari/pattidari) often collectively managed revenue. The British encountered this complexity and sought simplification — driven by the need for predictable, measurable revenue rather than by any concern for peasant welfare.

#### 2. Permanent Settlement (1793) — Bengal, Bihar, Orissa
**Architect:** Lord Cornwallis; implemented by John Shore.
**Mechanism:** Zamindars (revenue farmers under the Mughals) were declared perpetual hereditary proprietors of land, with a fixed revenue demand set permanently with the colonial state. If zamindars failed to pay by the "Sunset Law" deadline, their estates could be auctioned.
**Theory:** The colonial state believed that secure property rights would motivate zamindars to invest in improving their land — echoing the English landlord model.
**Reality:**
- Zamindars had no incentive to improve land; they simply rack-rented peasants (sub-tenants, raiyats).
- The fixed revenue demand, while good for zamindars initially, did not allow the state to benefit from rising agricultural productivity — unlike the Ryotwari areas.
- Peasants had no security of tenure; sub-infeudation created multiple layers between peasant and zamindar.
- Many zamindars were absentee landlords in Calcutta, disconnected from agricultural management.
- The Bengal famine of 1943 has roots traceable to the landlordism entrenched by the Permanent Settlement.
**Beneficiaries:** Zamindars (became a new landed gentry); Company (assured revenue); Money-lenders (who financed zamindars facing Sunset Law auctions).
**Losers:** Peasants (lost customary tenure rights, subjected to rack-renting).

#### 3. Ryotwari System — Madras and Bombay
**Architects:** Captain Alexander Read and Thomas Munro (Madras); refined by Elphinstone (Bombay).
**Mechanism:** Each individual cultivating peasant (ryot) was assessed directly by the state. Revenue demand was based on the assessed productivity of each plot. Settlements were revised periodically (every 20–30 years).
**Theory:** The Ryotwari system was presented as direct, fair, and removing the middleman (zamindar).
**Reality:**
- Revenue assessments were frequently excessive — often 50–60% of gross produce.
- Periodic revisions allowed the state to increase demands as land productivity improved.
- Failed harvests led to peasant debt since the revenue demand was inflexible.
- Created a class of indebted small peasants vulnerable to moneylenders.
- In Deccan, the Deccan Riots (1875) — peasants burning moneylenders' bonds and account books — were the direct result of Ryotwari exploitation combined with moneylender penetration.
- The Deccan Agriculturists' Relief Act (1879) was a belated British response, attempting to regulate moneylender power.

#### 4. Mahalwari System — NW Provinces and Punjab
**Architect:** Holt Mackenzie (1822); later refined by Robert Bird and James Thomason.
**Mechanism:** Revenue was assessed on the village community (*mahal*) as a whole. The village collectively paid revenue, which was then apportioned among cultivators.
**Nature:** Hybrid system — it recognized village community as revenue unit but still imposed a fixed monetary demand.
**Impact:** Better suited to north Indian village tradition, but like other systems, imposed cash revenue demands in a subsistence economy — forcing peasants into the market and into debt.

#### 5. Punjab Land Alienation Act (1900)
A significant piece of agrarian legislation — passed under Lord Curzon — which prohibited non-agricultural castes (primarily moneylenders) from permanently acquiring agricultural land. This was the colonial state's belated recognition that its own revenue systems had created a moneylender-induced dispossession of the peasantry in Punjab. While it protected agricultural land, it did not address the fundamental problems of high revenue demands and peasant debt.

#### 6. Famines as Structural Outcome
Colonial revenue policy drove famines. The key connection: (a) Revenue was collected in cash, not produce, forcing peasants to sell crops regardless of price; (b) High fixed revenue left no peasant surplus for food security; (c) British free-trade policy allowed food exports even during famines (notably 1876–78 and 1943); (d) The Great Bengal Famine of 1943 — 2–3 million deaths — occurred with food exports continuing from India to the war effort. Mike Davis (*Late Victorian Holocausts*) and Utsa Patnaik have documented the lethal link between colonial economic policy and mass mortality.

#### 7. Agrarian Legislation — Limited Colonial Reforms
- **Bengal Tenancy Act (1885):** Gave some security of tenure to long-term occupancy raiyats in Bengal — a response to peasant pressure and nationalist criticism. However, its implementation was weak.
- **Deccan Agriculturists' Relief Act (1879):** Attempted to regulate moneylender practices in Maharashtra.
- **Punjab Land Alienation Act (1900):** Protected agricultural land from moneylender acquisition.
These acts were reactive, not structural, and did not challenge the fundamental exploitative character of colonial revenue policy.

#### 8. Nationalist Economic Critique
**Romesh Chandra Dutt** in *Economic History of India* (2 vols., 1902) systematically documented how colonial revenue policy impoverished India's peasantry. **Dadabhai Naoroji** connected it to the Drain of Wealth — revenues extracted from land were ultimately remitted to Britain. **G.K. Gokhale** raised these issues in the Imperial Legislative Council. This economic critique directly fed into nationalist politics — making economic grievance the fuel of the freedom struggle.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** The agrarian distress created by colonial revenue systems was the material foundation of Indian nationalist politics. It is no coincidence that Gandhi's first Indian campaign — Champaran (1917) — was agrarian; the peasantry had been politicised by a century of colonial extraction.

**Conclusion 2 (Legacy):** Post-independence India's first legislative priority was Zamindari Abolition (1950s) and land reform — a direct response to the landlordism created by the Permanent Settlement. The colonial revenue legacy thus shaped the constitutional and legislative agenda of free India.

**Conclusion 3 (Analytical):** Colonial revenue systems reveal the fundamental contradiction of British rule — it was presented as "modernising" but it systematically destroyed the economic base of the peasantry through extraction, creating the poverty that British apologists later attributed to "traditional" Indian backwardness.

# MICROTHEME 3
## Economic Impact of Colonialism: Deindustrialisation and Drain of Wealth

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Colonialism was not merely political subjugation — it was economic transformation. British rule systematically dismantled India's pre-existing artisanal and manufacturing industries, converted India into a supplier of raw materials and a market for British manufactures, and extracted a significant portion of India's wealth through the "Drain" — laying the foundation of India's underdevelopment.

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** Before British conquest, India was one of the world's leading manufacturing economies. Dacca muslin, Bengal silk, Surat cotton, and Mysore iron were exported globally. By the mid-19th century, Indian artisanal industry had been largely destroyed — not by "natural" market forces but by deliberate colonial policy: differential tariffs, railway integration favouring British goods, and the destruction of court patronage.

**Introduction 3 (Economist's perspective):** Dadabhai Naoroji's *Poverty and Un-British Rule in India* (1901) and Romesh Chandra Dutt's *Economic History of India* (1902) first systematically articulated what modern economists now call "colonial exploitation" — India transferred between £200–500 million annually to Britain through trade tribute, Home Charges, and services, with no corresponding benefit.

**Introduction 4 (Quote-based):** As Jawaharlal Nehru wrote in *The Discovery of India*: "The British connected India with the world market but as a junior and exploited partner. The railroads that they built served to carry raw materials to the ports and British goods to the interior, not to develop Indian industry."

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Pre-Colonial Indian Economy
India's pre-colonial economy was not "primitive." In 1700, India accounted for approximately 24% of world GDP (Angus Maddison's estimates). Indian textiles — particularly Bengal muslin, Gujarat cotton, and Coromandel printed cloth — dominated Asian and even European luxury markets. Indian artisans possessed sophisticated technical knowledge: the Dacca muslin (*woven air*) was so fine that a full-length sari could pass through a finger ring. India also had substantial iron-smelting traditions (wootz steel from South India was globally valued), shipbuilding industries (Bombay dockyard built EIC ships), and a sophisticated banking and credit system (hundis, shroffs, seths).

#### 2. Deindustrialisation: The Mechanism
Deindustrialisation — the destruction of India's artisanal manufacturing base — occurred through multiple colonial mechanisms:

**Tariff Policy:** British manufacturers faced Indian competition in their home market. Manchester cotton mill owners lobbied Parliament. Result: Indian textiles were charged 70–80% import duties in Britain, while British textiles entered India at 2.5% (and cotton duties imposed in India in 1894 were immediately counter-balanced by excise duties to protect Lancashire). This was "free trade" imposed only on India.

**Destruction of Court Patronage:** Indian ruling courts — Mughal, Nawabi, Maratha — had been the primary patrons of luxury artisanal production. British conquest eliminated these courts (or converted them into pensioners), destroying the primary market for fine craftsmanship. The Dacca muslin industry collapsed most dramatically — Dacca's population fell from 150,000 to 30,000 by 1840 as weavers lost their livelihood.

**Railway Integration:** Railways, introduced from 1853, were presented as "modernisation." But their route structure was designed to link raw material production areas (cotton, jute, indigo) to coastal ports for export — not to connect Indian production centres to Indian consumption centres. They facilitated the inflow of cheap British manufactured goods into the Indian interior.

**Machine Competition:** British industrial revolution produced cheap machine-made textiles that undercut Indian handloom production in price. Indian weavers, already disadvantaged by differential tariffs, could not compete. The handloom sector contracted massively — though it never completely disappeared (notably in silk and specialty textiles).

#### 3. Regional Dimensions of Deindustrialisation
- **Bengal/Dacca:** Dacca muslin was the most famous casualty. The legend (disputed) of fingers being cut — actually reflected the collapse of market and patronage, not mutilation.
- **Surat:** Formerly India's most important port and textile centre; declined as British trade displaced Surat merchants.
- **Bihar:** Indigo was imposed on Bihar's peasants — the indigo plantation system (nij and ryoti cultivation) forced peasants to grow indigo for European planters at below-market rates. The **Indigo Revolt (Nil Bidroha, 1859–60)** was the peasant response; **Neel Darpan** (Dinabandhu Mitra, 1858) dramatised their exploitation and was translated into English by Rev. Long — who was convicted for defamation by the British courts, exposing the racial nature of colonial justice.

#### 4. The Drain of Wealth Theory
**Dadabhai Naoroji** (1825–1917) — called the "Grand Old Man of India" and the first Indian Member of the British Parliament (1892) — developed the Drain theory most systematically. The "Drain" consisted of:
- **Home Charges:** Annual payments from India to Britain for salaries of India Office staff, pensions of retired colonial officials, interest on loans taken for Indian railways (but profiting British bondholders), cost of Indian Office in London — estimated at £30–40 million annually.
- **Trade Surplus without Capital Import:** India consistently ran a trade surplus (exported more than it imported), but the surplus was not retained in India as capital — it was remitted as tribute. Unlike European countries' colonial surpluses which funded industrialisation, India's surplus funded British industrialisation.
- **Invisible Transfers:** Freight, insurance, and banking charges paid to British firms even for India's own trade.

Naoroji estimated India's annual drain at £30 million in the 1870s; William Digby (*Prosperous British India*, 1901) put it higher. Modern estimates by Utsa Patnaik suggest the total drain over colonial rule was $45 trillion (in 2018 dollars) — though this remains debated.

**Romesh Chandra Dutt's** two-volume *Economic History of India* (1902) complemented Naoroji by tracing the policy history of deindustrialisation and agrarian impoverishment. **G.K. Gokhale** raised these issues in the Imperial Legislative Council.

**Mahadev Govind Ranade** contributed through "early political economy" — arguing India needed protective tariffs and industrial policy, not British free trade.

#### 5. Railways: Instrument of Exploitation or Modernisation?
The British built 66,000 km of railway by 1947 — the world's fourth largest network. Nationalist critique:
- Capital was raised through guaranteed interest bonds paid by Indian taxpayers — risk was Indian, profit was British.
- Railway equipment was imported from Britain, not manufactured in India — no industrial multiplier effect.
- Route structure was extractive, not developmental (ports, not production centres).
- However, railways also created a national market, facilitated grain movement during famines, and became the infrastructure on which post-independence industrialisation built.

#### 6. Rise of Indian Entrepreneurship
Despite colonial obstacles, some Indian entrepreneurship emerged:
- **Jamsetji Nusserwanji Tata** established Tata Iron and Steel Company (TISCO) at Jamshedpur in 1907 — India's first major modern steel plant. This was a direct act of economic nationalism against British claims that Indians could not industrialise.
- Indian cotton mill owners in Bombay and Ahmedabad competed with Lancashire.
- The Swadeshi Movement (1905–08) gave political legitimacy to Indian enterprise.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** The economic critique of colonialism pioneered by Naoroji and Dutt laid the intellectual foundation for the nationalist movement. Economic nationalism preceded political nationalism — Indians first needed to understand they were being impoverished before they could demand freedom.

**Conclusion 2 (Legacy):** Post-independence India's economic policies — import substitution industrialisation, Five-Year Plans, public sector emphasis — were directly shaped by the colonial lesson that free trade in a structurally unequal context destroys the weaker partner. The "Licence Raj" was as much a response to colonial deindustrialisation as it was socialist ideology.

# MICROTHEME 4
## The Revolt of 1857: Causes, Course, and Consequences

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The Revolt of 1857 was the most significant challenge to British colonial rule in the 19th century. What began as a sepoy mutiny at Meerut on May 10, 1857, rapidly expanded into a widespread uprising that drew in dispossessed princes, aggrieved zamindars, displaced artisans, and discontented peasants — transforming a military mutiny into, in the words of V.D. Savarkar, "The First War of Indian Independence."

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** The Revolt of 1857 was the culmination of a hundred years of recurrent local resistances — Sanyasi and Fakir rebellions, Vellore Mutiny (1806), Kittoor revolt (1824), Kittur Rani Chennamma — all rooted in colonial economic disruption and cultural disrespect. 1857 was when these accumulated grievances converged.

**Introduction 3 (Historiographical):** Few events in Indian history have generated as much historiographical debate as 1857. For British historians (J.W. Kaye, Malleson), it was a "Sepoy Mutiny." For Indian nationalists (V.D. Savarkar), it was the "First War of Independence." For Marxist historians (S.N. Sen, R.C. Majumdar), it was a feudal reaction. For subaltern historians, it was a popular uprising with its own logic, not merely a derivative of elite politics.

**Introduction 4 (Constitutional):** The Revolt of 1857 is a watershed in constitutional history — it ended 100 years of Company rule and transferred governance to the British Crown under the Government of India Act (1858), fundamentally restructuring the institutional architecture of colonial rule.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Underlying Causes — The Century of Grievances

**Political Causes:**
- The Doctrine of Lapse (Dalhousie) had dispossessed legitimate rulers of Jhansi, Nagpur, Satara, Awadh. Nana Sahib, adopted son of the last Peshwa, was denied his pension. Rani Lakshmibai of Jhansi was denied the right to adopt a heir. These directly led to their participation in 1857.
- Annexation of Awadh (1856) on grounds of "misgovernance" was deeply resented — Awadh was the cultural heartland, and its Nawab Wajid Ali Shah (a patron of arts) was exiled to Calcutta.

**Economic Causes:**
- Destruction of artisan industries (see Microtheme 3) had created unemployed artisans (particularly weavers) who joined the revolt.
- Zamindars whose estates had been seized under revenue laws (especially in Awadh and NW Provinces) were ready rebels.
- Colonial revenue policy had impoverished peasantry — providing a mass base.

**Social and Religious Causes:**
- Fear of forced conversion: Missionary activity, combined with legal reform (Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act, 1856), was interpreted as an attack on traditional Hindu/Muslim social order.
- The **General Service Enlistment Act (1856):** Required sepoys to serve overseas if required. For Hindus, crossing the sea (kala pani) meant ritual pollution and loss of caste — a profound religious grievance.
- **Caste grievances:** Mixed-caste regiments; Brahmin sepoys forced to march alongside lower-caste soldiers.

**Military Causes:**
- The Bengal Army was disproportionately composed of high-caste Hindus (Rajputs, Brahmins) from Awadh, who had close links to the annexed kingdom.
- British officers' racial arrogance had eroded trust.
- Better pay for British officers; poor career prospects for Indian sepoys.

**Immediate Cause:**
The **cartridge controversy:** The Enfield rifle (introduced 1856) used greased cartridges whose ends had to be bitten off before loading. Rumour spread — never fully refuted — that the grease was a mixture of cow fat (sacred to Hindus) and pig lard (forbidden to Muslims). This was interpreted as a deliberate British attempt to destroy caste and religious identity.

#### 2. The Course of the Revolt

**March 29, 1857:** Mangal Pandey, sepoy at Barrackpore cantonment, attacked his British officers and called on his regiment to revolt. He was arrested, tried, and hanged (April 8, 1857) — becoming the revolt's first martyr.

**May 10, 1857 (Meerut):** Eighty-five sepoys of the 3rd Light Cavalry had refused to use the new cartridges and been court-martialled. On May 10, their fellow sepoys rose in revolt, freed the prisoners, killed British officers, and marched to Delhi.

**May 11, 1857 (Delhi):** The sepoys arrived at Delhi, where Bahadur Shah Zafar — the last Mughal Emperor, then 82 years old — was persuaded (reluctantly) to become the symbolic leader of the revolt. Delhi became the epicentre. Its fall gave the revolt both a constitutional legitimacy (Mughal sovereignty) and a pan-Indian symbol.

**Spread:** The revolt spread rapidly to:
- **Lucknow:** Begum Hazrat Mahal led resistance; Birjis Qadr (her son) was proclaimed Nawab. The British Residency was besieged for 87 days.
- **Kanpur (Cawnpore):** Nana Sahib Peshwa (Dhondu Pant) led the uprising. British garrison surrendered; a controversial massacre of British captives occurred.
- **Jhansi:** Rani Lakshmibai refused to submit; fought brilliantly before being defeated. She died fighting at Gwalior (June 1858) — described by the British General Hugh Rose as "the best and bravest" of the rebel leaders.
- **Bareilly:** Khan Bahadur Khan proclaimed Nawab of Rohilkhand.
- **Bihar:** Kunwar Singh of Jagdishpur — an 80-year-old zamindar — led a remarkably effective guerrilla campaign.
- **Tantya Tope:** Nana Sahib's commander, who escaped and conducted guerrilla warfare until 1859.

**Areas of Non-Revolt:** Punjab, Sind, Rajputana, Hyderabad, Madras, Bombay — either recently conquered (Punjab), or with social dynamics that kept them loyal, or with native rulers who actively supported the British.

#### 3. British Suppression
British response was swift and brutal. John Lawrence in Punjab mobilized loyal Sikh forces (who had old grievances against the Mughal-Maratha combine). Delhi was recaptured on September 14–20, 1857 — Bahadur Shah Zafar was captured, his sons killed, and he was exiled to Rangoon where he died in 1862. Lucknow was relieved and then recaptured (March 1858). Lakshmibai was defeated; Tantya Tope was eventually betrayed and hanged (April 1859). By mid-1858, organised resistance had collapsed.

#### 4. Consequences and Aftermath

**Constitutional:**
- **Government of India Act (1858):** Abolished the East India Company; transferred all powers to the British Crown. India was now directly governed by a Secretary of State for India (with a Council) and a Viceroy replaced the Governor-General. The Company's Board of Control was abolished.
- **Queen's Proclamation (November 1, 1858):** Promised: (a) no further annexations; (b) respect for treaties with Indian princes; (c) religious tolerance; (d) equal opportunity for Indians in government service (largely unimplemented). This became the constitutional charter of Crown rule.

**Military:** The Bengal Army was reorganised. Indian units were reduced; British troops increased. Divide-and-rule in military recruitment — "martial races" theory introduced, favouring Sikhs, Gurkhas, Jats, Pathans (who had been "loyal" in 1857) and systematically excluding Bengalis, Biharis, and Awadhi sepoys.

**Policy Shifts:** British policy became more cautious: no more doctrine of lapse; respect for Indian customs and religion (officially); reliance on loyal princes as "breakwaters" against nationalist tides.

**Social:** The revolt widened the racial gulf between Indians and Britons. Social mixing declined further; racial segregation in cantonments increased.

#### 5. Historiographical Debate
- **British view** (contemporary): "Sepoy Mutiny" — a military disorder caused by religious panic.
- **V.D. Savarkar** (1909): "First War of Independence" — a nationally conscious revolt with clear political objectives. This view became the Congress/nationalist mainstream.
- **R.C. Majumdar:** Largely agreed with British — called it neither first, nor national, nor war of independence.
- **S.N. Sen** (*Eighteen Fifty-Seven*, commissioned by GoI, 1957): Balanced — sepoy mutiny that became a popular revolt in some areas.
- **Subaltern historians (Eric Stokes, Ranajit Guha):** Focussed on the autonomous role of peasants, tribals — rejecting both the "mutiny" and "war of independence" frameworks.
- **Bipan Chandra:** Emphasised the consciousness of Hindu-Muslim unity during 1857 — both communities fought together under Mughal and other symbols.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Watershed):** The 1857 Revolt was a decisive watershed — it ended the Company era, forced a restructuring of British policy, and planted in Indian memory a tradition of anti-colonial resistance that later nationalists consciously invoked. Without 1857, the 1947 story is incomplete.

**Conclusion 2 (Nation-building):** Despite its ultimate failure, the 1857 Revolt demonstrated that Indians of different religions, castes, and regions could unite against colonial rule — a lesson that Gandhi and later nationalist leaders built upon. The Hindu-Muslim cooperation of 1857 became a template and an aspiration for the freedom movement.

**Conclusion 3 (Legacy):** The British response to 1857 — "martial races" theory, racial segregation, reliance on loyal princes — created structural features of Indian colonial society that cast long shadows: communalism, regionalism, and the arms-length relationship between state and society that independent India had to overcome.

# MICROTHEME 5
## Tribal and Peasant Uprisings in Colonial India

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Long before the organised nationalist movement, India's tribal communities and peasants were in revolt against colonial disruption. These uprisings — from the Sanyasi Rebellion (1770s) to the Telangana Peasant Rebellion (1946–51) — were not mere law-and-order problems but expressions of profound social, economic, and cultural resistance to the transformation of subsistence communities into extractive colonial economies.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** Tribal revolts and peasant movements occupy distinct analytical space. Tribal revolts were driven by land alienation, colonial forest laws, and the penetration of "diku" (outsiders — traders, moneylenders, officials) into autonomous tribal economies. Peasant movements were driven by rack-renting, revenue pressure, and the destruction of traditional tenancy rights. Both converged on the theme of colonial dispossession.

**Introduction 3 (Historiographical):** Ranajit Guha's *Elementary Aspects of Peasant Insurgency in Colonial India* (1983) challenged the nationalist historiography that saw tribal and peasant revolts merely as precursors to the "real" national movement. Guha argued these movements had their own autonomous logic, ideology, and consciousness — not derivative of elite nationalism.

**Introduction 4 (Subaltern):** The tribals and peasants who fought colonial rule deserve historical recognition as agents in their own right, not merely as a "mass base" for nationalist leaders. Birsa Munda, Sidhu-Kanhu, Alluri Sitarama Raju — their struggles were the lived experience of anti-colonialism at the grass roots.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Early Tribal Uprisings (Pre-1857)

**Kol Uprising (1831–32, Jharkhand):** The Kols — an adivasi community — rose against the penetration of outsider zamindars, money-lenders, and traders (called "diku" — outsiders) into Chota Nagpur. The immediate triggers were forced labour and land alienation. Led by Buddhu Bhagat, the uprising was suppressed by the colonial military but left a legacy of adivasi consciousness.

**Santhal Hul (1855–56):** This is the most significant pre-1857 tribal uprising. The Santhals of the Damin-i-Koh region (Rajmahal Hills, present Jharkhand) — who had been settled there by the British to clear forests — revolted against the penetration of moneylenders and zamindars. Leaders **Sidhu and Kanhu Murmu** declared that "the thikadars (contractors), mahajans (moneylenders), and zamindars" were to be expelled, and that divine command (*Thakur jiu*) sanctioned the revolt. The uprising was crushed with artillery, but not before 10,000+ Santhals had died in what they believed was a divinely ordained liberation war. The British response included the Santhal Parganas Act (1855), creating a separate administrative unit with some protection — an acknowledgment that Santhal grievances were structural. *Historical significance:* The Santhal Hul had millenarian dimensions — belief in divine sanction for revolt against injustice — which makes it an autonomous ideological movement, not merely a reaction.

#### 2. Birsa Munda and the Ulgulan (1899–1900)
**Birsa Munda** (1875–1900) is the most iconic tribal leader of colonial India. Called *Dharat Abba* (Father of the Earth), he emerged in the Munda areas of Chota Nagpur as a prophet, healer, and organiser. The Mundas had a system called *khuntkatti* — community land ownership by the original settler families. The penetration of thekedars (contractors), dikus, and the introduction of colonial land laws undermined this.

Birsa launched what he called *Ulgulan* (Great Tumult/Revolution) in 1899–1900. His ideology synthesised: (a) demand for restoration of Munda land rights; (b) millenarian religious vision (he claimed divine powers); (c) explicit anti-colonial politics. The Ulgulan was suppressed; Birsa was arrested and died in Ranchi jail in June 1900, aged 25. His legacy was enormous: the Chotanagpur Tenancy Act (1908) formally recognised *khuntkatti* rights — a direct result of the Ulgulan. Today, Birsa Munda is the only adivasi leader with his portrait in the Central Hall of Parliament, and his birth state is named **Jharkhand** ("Forest Land") — carrying his vision into constitutional geography.

#### 3. Other Tribal Uprisings
- **Bhil Uprising (Rajasthan/Maharashtra, multiple, 1818–1913):** Against British revenue extraction and loss of forest rights.
- **Rampa Rebellion (1922–24, Andhra Pradesh):** Led by **Alluri Sitarama Raju** in the Rampa (Godavari) Agency hills. The Forest Regulations that restricted the traditional practice of *podu* (shifting cultivation) were the trigger. Alluri — a charismatic leader who merged Gandhi's non-cooperation ideas with armed resistance — was killed in 1924. Commemorated in AP; featured in the film *RRR* (2022) — raising his historical profile.
- **Moplah Rebellion (1921, Malabar):** Complex — partly a peasant revolt (Muslim tenant farmers against Hindu landlords), partly a communal episode, partly a response to the Non-Cooperation/Khilafat Movement. Its characterisation remains contested — nationalist, communal, or class revolt?

#### 4. Peasant Movements — Colonial Period

**Indigo Revolt / Nil Bidroha (1859–60, Bengal):** Bengal peasants rose against the indigo plantation system where European planters forced them to cultivate indigo (nij cultivation on planters' own land, or ryoti cultivation on peasant land) at exploitative rates. The Bengal peasantry refused to grow indigo and resisted with remarkable organisation. *Neel Darpan* by Dinabandhu Mitra (1858) dramatised their plight — it was translated by Rev. James Long into English; Long's subsequent conviction for libel exposed colonial justice. An Indigo Commission (1860) was appointed; indigo cultivation in Bengal eventually declined.

**Deccan Riots (1875, Maharashtra):** Peasants in Poona and Ahmednagar districts rose against moneylenders, burning their account books and demand-notes. This was a specific protest against the bond of debt — the weapon of the moneylender — rather than against landlordism. The Deccan Agriculturists' Relief Act (1879) was the colonial response.

**Peasant Movements linked to Gandhi:** Gandhi's three early Indian campaigns were all peasant-focused:
- **Champaran Satyagraha (1917):** Against the indigo tinkathia system (requiring peasants to grow indigo on 3/20 of their land for planters). Gandhi's arrival and investigation led to the Champaran Agrarian Act (1918) abolishing the tinkathia system. Gandhi's first application of satyagraha in India.
- **Kheda Satyagraha (1918):** Peasants in Kheda district, Gujarat, demanded remission of land revenue following crop failure. Gandhi and Patel led the campaign; the government finally suspended collection.
- **Bardoli Satyagraha (1928):** Vallabhbhai Patel led Bardoli peasants against a 30% revenue enhancement. The campaign was a model of nonviolent, disciplined resistance; the revenue increase was ultimately revised. Patel earned the title *Sardar* (Leader) here.

#### 5. Later Peasant and Agrarian Movements (1930s–40s)

**All India Kisan Sabha (1936):** Founded with Swami Sahajanand Saraswati as President and N.G. Ranga as Secretary. Brought peasant politics into organised form under socialist-left influence. Demanded abolition of zamindari, reduction of rent, fair wages for agricultural labourers.

**Tebhaga Movement (1946, Bengal):** Bargadars (sharecroppers) demanded two-thirds share of produce (*tebhaga* = three shares, two for the cultivator). Led by the Kisan Sabha (communist-influenced). Suppressed, but prefigured post-independence land reform.

**Telangana Peasant Rebellion (1946–51):** The most sustained and radical peasant uprising in colonial and post-colonial India. In the princely state of Hyderabad, the Communist Party of India (CPI) organised the peasants of Nalgonda, Warangal, and Khammam against the feudal vetti (forced labour) system and the deshmukh-deshpande landlord class. Guerrilla units (*dalam*) redistributed land. The Indian Army entered Hyderabad in September 1948 (Operation Polo) primarily to end Nizam rule; the CPI continued the rebellion until 1951. The movement demonstrated the radical potential of organised agrarian struggle.

**Punnapra-Vayalar Uprising (1946, Travancore):** Socialist-led uprising of coir workers and peasants in Travancore against the Dewan's authoritarian rule. Suppressed with considerable loss of life — but it showed that even in "native states," colonial-linked feudalism faced organised resistance.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** Tribal and peasant uprisings were not peripheral to the freedom struggle — they were its subterranean foundations. When Gandhi arrived in Champaran in 1917, he was building on a century of peasant resistance that had prepared the ground. Independent India's commitment to social justice — through the Fifth and Sixth Schedules, Panchayati Raj, and land reform — reflects this legacy.

**Conclusion 3 (Contemporary):** Issues of tribal land alienation, forest rights (Forest Rights Act, 2006), and agrarian distress in India today have direct genealogical connections to the colonial disruptions that sparked these 19th and early 20th century uprisings. The historical and the contemporary are inseparable.

# MICROTHEME 6
## Socio-Religious Reform Movements (19th–Early 20th Century)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** The 19th century in India was an era of social awakening. The encounter with Western liberal thought, the challenge of Christian missionary critique, and the disruptions of colonial modernity together pushed Indian intellectuals to critically examine their own social traditions — producing a rich and diverse current of socio-religious reform that laid the cultural foundations for modern India.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** Socio-religious reform movements were not monolithic. They ranged from rationalist reformism (Rammohun Roy) to revivalist nationalism (Dayanand Saraswati), from universal humanitarianism (Vivekananda) to radical anti-caste activism (Jyotirao Phule, Ambedkar), and from modernist Islamic thought (Aligarh Movement) to Islamic revivalism (Deoband). Their diversity reflects the complexity of 19th-century Indian society's encounter with colonial modernity.

**Introduction 3 (Historical):** Beginning with Raja Rammohun Roy's campaign against sati and the formation of the Brahmo Samaj (1828), socio-religious reform movements produced four major outcomes: legislative change (banning of sati, widow remarriage act), institutional change (new educational institutions), political consciousness (feeding into nationalism), and ideological debate (social reform vs. revivalism).

**Introduction 4 (Constitutional):** The socio-religious reform movements of the 19th and early 20th century directly shaped the fundamental rights and directive principles of India's Constitution — particularly provisions on gender equality, untouchability, and religious freedom. The Constitution is, in part, the legislative culmination of the reform movements.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Bengal Renaissance and Brahmo Samaj

**Raja Rammohun Roy (1772–1833)** — the "Father of Modern India" — was the first major reformer. His significance:
- Founded the **Brahmo Samaj** (1828) — a monotheistic reformist movement that rejected polytheism, idol worship, and caste discrimination.
- Campaigned against **sati** — resulting in the Bengal Sati Regulation (1829) under Governor-General Bentinck. This was the first colonial social reform legislation, secured through the partnership of Indian reformer and colonial state.
- Advocated English education as the path to rational thought.
- Founded *Sambad Kaumudi* (1821, Bengali) and *Mirat-ul-Akhbar* (Persian) — pioneering nationalist journalism.
- Visited Britain (1831) — the first Indian reformer to take the reform argument to the colonial metropole.

**Henry Louis Vivian Derozio (1809–31)** — Eurasian teacher at Hindu College — inspired the **Young Bengal Movement**: a rationalist, free-thinking current that challenged traditional authority, religion, and caste. Though short-lived (Derozio was dismissed in 1831), it planted seeds of scientific rationalism.

**Brahmo Samaj's evolution:** Under **Debendranath Tagore** (Tattvabodhini Sabha, 1839), it became more systematically theistic. Under **Keshub Chunder Sen**, it split — one faction took a more syncretic approach, another focused on social reform including widow remarriage (Civil Marriage Act, 1872). The Brahmo movement ultimately influenced Indian social thought more than its limited membership suggests.

**Ishwarchandra Vidyasagar (1820–1891):** Not a Brahmo but a reformer of equal significance — championed **widow remarriage**, resulting in the Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act (1856). Also promoted women's education (founding 35+ schools in Bengal), opposed child marriage, and wrote foundational Bengali textbooks (*Barna Parichay*). A rationalist who worked within Hindu tradition to reform it.

#### 2. Arya Samaj
**Swami Dayanand Saraswati (1824–1883)** founded the **Arya Samaj** in Bombay in 1875. His approach differed fundamentally from Brahmo Samaj:
- Grounded in Vedic authority (*Back to the Vedas*) — not Western rationalism.
- *Satyarth Prakash* (1875) — his foundational text — argued the Vedas contained all truth and that subsequent Hindu religion had been corrupted by priestly monopoly, idol worship, and caste.
- Championed **Shuddhi** (purification/reconversion) — to reconvert those who had converted to Islam or Christianity.
- Strongly opposed child marriage, sati, and purdah.
- Founded **DAV (Dayanand Anglo-Vedic) schools and colleges** — creating an educational network that spread nationalism through modern education with a Hindu cultural identity.
- Significance for nationalism: Arya Samaj's emphasis on Hindu cultural pride, its educational network, and its challenge to British missionary activity made it a seedbed of militant nationalism — particularly in Punjab. Lala Lajpat Rai and Bal Gangadhar Tilak were both influenced by it (Tilak less directly).

#### 3. Ramakrishna Mission
**Ramakrishna Paramhansa (1836–1886)** — mystic saint of Bengal — taught that all religions lead to the same God; his life was a practical demonstration of *jnana*, *bhakti*, and *karma* yoga. He did not found an organisation himself.

**Swami Vivekananda (1863–1902)** was Ramakrishna's chief disciple and the organiser of his message. Vivekananda founded the **Ramakrishna Mission** (1897) — combining Hindu spirituality with social service. His significance:
- **Chicago Parliament of Religions (1893):** His opening address — "Sisters and Brothers of America" — received a two-minute standing ovation and introduced Vedanta to the West. More significantly, it restored Indian civilisational pride at a moment of colonial cultural subordination.
- Articulated *practical Vedanta*: service to the poor as service to God (*Daridra Narayana*). This fused social activism with religious philosophy.
- Inspired a generation of nationalists — including Bal Gangadhar Tilak, Aurobindo Ghosh, and indirectly Subhas Chandra Bose.
- His message: "We want muscles of iron and nerves of steel" — a direct counter to colonial stereotyping of Indians as effeminate.

#### 4. Prarthana Samaj (Maharashtra)
Founded in 1867 in Bombay, influenced by the Brahmo Samaj but with a specifically Maharashtrian character. Led by **Justice Mahadev Govind Ranade** — also the founder of Indian political economy. Prarthana Samaj focussed on: widow remarriage, opposition to caste discrimination, women's education. Less dramatic than Arya Samaj but significant for integrating religious reform with liberal economics.

#### 5. Reform of Gender: The Women's Question
The "women's question" — centred on sati, widow remarriage, women's education, purdah, and child marriage — was central to 19th-century reform.

- **Pandita Ramabai (1858–1922):** An extraordinary figure — a Sanskrit scholar, a Brahmin widow who converted to Christianity, and founder of the **Mukti Mission** providing shelter and education to Hindu widows. Her *The High-Caste Hindu Woman* (1887) was a searing indictment of the social condition of Hindu women.
- **Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain (1880–1932):** Bengali Muslim author and activist; *Sultana's Dream* (1905) was a feminist utopia; founded the Sakhawat Memorial Girls' School (1909). First major Muslim feminist voice.
- **Child Marriage Restraint Act (1929) / Sarda Act:** The first legal restriction on child marriage — fixing minimum age at 14 for girls and 18 for boys. Inadequately enforced but symbolically significant.

#### 6. Anti-Caste Reform
The most radical strand of 19th–20th century social reform challenged caste — not just reformed upper-caste practices.

**Jyotirao Phule (1827–1890):** Maharashtra; Shudra caste background. Founded **Satyashodhak Samaj** (Truth-Seeking Society, 1873) — explicitly anti-Brahmin, arguing that Brahminism was an imposed system of exploitation masquerading as religion. Founded the first school for girls (1848) and for Dalits. His *Gulamgiri* (Slavery, 1873) dedicated to American abolitionists — the first explicit Indian connection between caste and slavery.

**E.V. Ramasamy Periyar (1879–1973):** Tamil Nadu. Founded the **Self-Respect Movement** (1925) — challenged both Brahminism and Congress's failure to address Brahmin dominance. Founded Dravida Kazhagam (DK). Advocated rationalism, atheism, and women's autonomy. His movement reshaped Tamil politics and directly produced the Dravidian parties that have governed Tamil Nadu since 1967.

**B.R. Ambedkar (1891–1956):** The most systematic thinker on caste. *Annihilation of Caste* (1936, undelivered address) argued caste could not be reformed from within Hinduism — it had to be annihilated. **Mahad Satyagraha (1927):** Led Dalits to publicly drink water from the Chawdar tank — asserting the right to public spaces. Burned the Manu Smriti. Led mass conversion to Buddhism (October 14, 1956, Nagpur) — over 500,000 people converted with him. Framed the debate between *Harijan uplift* (Gandhi's approach — working within the Hindu framework) and structural transformation (Ambedkar's demand).

**Ayyankali (1863–1941):** Kerala; led the **Pulaya** community (untouchables of Kerala) in demanding public road access, educational rights, and an end to vetti (forced labour). His methods included strikes of agricultural labourers — possibly the first such strike in India.

#### 7. Islamic Reform Movements

**Aligarh Movement:** Founded by **Sir Sayyid Ahmed Khan (1817–1898)** — the most influential Muslim intellectual of 19th-century India. After 1857 (in which he saw Muslim participation as a disaster for the community), he argued Muslims needed to acquire Western/English education to participate in colonial governance. Founded the **Muhammadan Anglo-Oriental (MAO) College** at Aligarh (1875) — later Aligarh Muslim University (1920). His journal *Tahzib-ul-Akhlaq* (Reforms of Morals) advocated social reform (against purdah, polygamy abuses). Crucially, his later career moved toward Muslim separatism — arguing Hindus and Muslims were two separate nations whose interests conflicted — an ideological seed of the Pakistan movement.

**Deoband Movement (1867):** Founded at Darul Uloom Deoband by Maulana Muhammad Qasim Nanotvi and Rashid Ahmad Gangohi. Reaction to British rule and Christian missionary activity — sought to preserve Islamic education and law through a network of madrasas (Islamic schools) modelled on British educational efficiency but focused on Islamic texts. Deoband produced nationalist ulama — the Jamiat-ul-Ulama-i-Hind (1919) was Deoband-affiliated and participated in the Khilafat-Non-Cooperation movement, opposing Partition.

**Faraizi Movement:** Founded by **Haji Shariat Ullah** (1781–1840) in Bengal — called for return to pure Faraiz (obligatory duties of Islam). His son **Dudu Mian** gave it a more explicitly anti-landlord and anti-colonial character. The Faraizi movement organised Bengal's Muslim peasantry against indigo planters and zamindars — combining religious revivalism with economic protest.

#### 8. Sikh Reform: Singh Sabha and Akali Movement
**Singh Sabha Movement (1873, Amritsar):** Response to Christian missionary activity and the proliferation of Hindu *sadhu* influence in Sikhism. Sought to define and purify Sikh identity — leading to the compilation of *Gurmat Granth Sahib* theology and the standardisation of Sikh practices. Educational institutions (Khalsa College, 1892) were key outcomes.

**Akali Movement (1920s):** Demanded Sikh control over historical *gurdwaras* (Sikh temples) that were under the control of corrupt *mahants* (priests). The **Sikh Gurdwaras Act (1925)** transferred gurdwara management to the elected Shiromani Gurdwara Parbandhak Committee (SGPC). This was simultaneously a religious reform and a political victory for the Sikh community against both colonial power and priestly abuse.

#### 9. Theosophical Society
Founded in New York (1875) by Helena Petrovna Blavatsky and Henry Steel Olcott; arrived in India in 1879. **Annie Besant** (1847–1933) — Irish-born — became the dominant figure in Indian Theosophy. Theosophists were unusual in their respect for Indian religions and philosophy (Hinduism, Buddhism) as equal or superior to Christianity — at a time when the colonial framework dismissed Indian religion. Theosophists supported Indian education (Central Hindu School, Varanasi — later Banaras Hindu University), revived public interest in Sanskrit learning, and contributed to cultural nationalism. Annie Besant was the **first woman INC President (1917)** and founded the **Home Rule League (Madras, 1916)** — connecting social and cultural work to political nationalism.

# MICROTHEME 7
## Colonial Education Policy and Indian Response

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Colonial education policy was not neutral — it was a deliberate instrument of cultural hegemony. The goal, articulated most bluntly by Thomas Babington Macaulay in 1835, was to create "a class of persons, Indian in blood and colour, but English in taste, in opinions, in morals, and in intellect." Understanding this goal is essential for understanding both the successes and failures of colonial education.

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** The Orientalism-Anglicism controversy (1813–35) was the first significant debate about Indian education under colonialism. The Orientalists (William Jones, H.H. Wilson) advocated support for traditional Indian learning in Persian, Sanskrit, and Arabic. The Anglicists (Macaulay, Trevelyan) argued for English-medium, Western-knowledge education. Macaulay's Minute (1835) settled the debate — in favour of Anglicism — with consequences that shaped India's intellectual and political life for two centuries.

**Introduction 3 (Constitutional):** The national education movement during the Swadeshi era, Gandhi's Nai Talim, and Tagore's Visva-Bharati were all critiques of colonial education that informed the constitutional vision of education — particularly the directive principles on free and compulsory education and the right to mother-tongue instruction.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Orientalism-Anglicism Debate (1813–1835)
**Orientalists:** Led by William Jones (founder, Asiatic Society of Bengal, 1784), H.H. Wilson, and H.T. Colebrooke. Argued that traditional Indian learning — Sanskrit, Persian, Arabic — had scientific and cultural value and that the colonial state should support existing Indian educational institutions. This was both intellectually sincere and politically convenient — Orientalism also produced knowledge that enabled governance (revenue records, legal codes, census categories).

**Anglicists:** Led by Macaulay (Law Member, Governor-General's Council) and Charles Trevelyan. Argued that Indian literature and science were inferior to European; that English education would modernise India; and that it was cost-effective (education a thin layer of English-educated Indians who would then spread knowledge "downward" — the "filtration theory").

**Macaulay's Minute (February 2, 1835):** The decisive document. Macaulay declared Sanskrit and Arabic literature "inferior" to European; that one shelf of European books was worth the entire literature of India and Arabia. Governor-General Bentinck accepted the Minute, and English was made the medium of instruction for higher education.

**Consequences:** English-medium education produced the Indian middle class — the social base of the nationalist movement. But it also created a cultural rupture — educated Indians became alienated from their vernacular traditions while being excluded from genuine equality with the British. This "colonial consciousness" was the psychological condition that nationalism had to overcome.

#### 2. Wood's Education Despatch (1854)
Often called the "Magna Carta of Indian Education." Secretary of State Charles Wood's Despatch proposed:
- A hierarchy of education: vernacular primary schools → English-medium high schools → universities.
- Universities in Bombay, Calcutta, and Madras (founded 1857) — the first affiliating universities in India.
- Department of Public Instruction in each province.
- Grants-in-aid to private schools.
- Women's education to be promoted.
The Despatch did not fundamentally challenge English-medium dominance — it simply organised the system more comprehensively.

#### 3. Hunter Commission (1882)
Appointed under W.W. Hunter. Recommended expansion of primary education; more autonomy to provinces; greater role for private (missionary and Indian) educational institutions. Significant for recognising that the colonial state had neglected mass (primary) education in favour of elite English education — a critique that nationalists developed further.

#### 4. Nationalist Critique of Colonial Education
The educated Indians produced by colonial education turned the tools of Western rationalism against the colonial system itself. But they also critiqued the education:

- **Impractical:** Produced "pen-pushers" for government jobs, not citizens, farmers, or skilled workers.
- **Denationalising:** Cut educated Indians off from their cultural roots; created self-contempt.
- **Unequal access:** Women, lower castes, and rural Indians had minimal access.
- **Irrelevant:** India's actual problems — agriculture, public health, industry — were not taught.

**Gopal Krishna Gokhale** repeatedly introduced a Compulsory Primary Education Bill in the Imperial Legislative Council (1911 etc.) — arguing India needed mass literacy, not just elite English education. The bills were repeatedly defeated.

#### 5. National Education Movement (Swadeshi Era)
During the Swadeshi movement (1905–08), after the Partition of Bengal, nationalists boycotted government-controlled schools and set up national educational institutions:
- **National Council of Education, Bengal (1906):** With Aurobindo Ghosh as Principal of the National College.
- Nationalist schools emphasising Indian history, vernacular languages, and physical education.
These institutions were often short-lived but established the principle that education should be in service of national consciousness, not colonial administration.

#### 6. Tagore's Visva-Bharati and Educational Philosophy
**Rabindranath Tagore (1861–1941)** founded **Visva-Bharati** at Santiniketan (1921) — a university that embodied his educational vision: learning in an open environment (outdoors, under trees), integration of arts with academics, synthesis of Indian and global culture, and freedom from rote learning. His essay *My School* critiqued the colonial school as a cage that destroyed children's natural curiosity. Tagore's educational philosophy was deeply influenced by ancient India's *gurukul* tradition while being fully modern in its global cultural outlook.

**Tagore vs. Gandhi on Nationalism:** While both were critics of colonial education, they differed on nationalism. Tagore was wary of a narrow, exclusive nationalism — he called it "the opium of the modern world." Gandhi's nationalism was rooted in the Indian village and religious tradition. This debate has contemporary relevance.

#### 7. Gandhi's Nai Talim (Basic/New Education)
Gandhi proposed **Nai Talim** (literally "New Education") in 1937 — also called **Basic Education** or **Wardha Scheme:**
- Education should be through craft (carpentry, spinning, agriculture, etc.) — learning by doing.
- Medium should be the mother tongue, not English.
- Education should be self-sustaining — the craft products pay for the school.
- Aim: educated citizens who could serve their village communities.
Nai Talim was incorporated into some Congress provincial government curricula (1937–39) but was never implemented at scale. Critics argued it was impractical and would consign rural Indians to rural occupations. Supporters argued it was the only education appropriate for India's village-based economy.

# MICROTHEME 8
## Emergence of Indian Nationalism and the Early Congress (1857–1905)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The emergence of organised Indian nationalism was a process that unfolded over several decades after 1857. Proto-nationalist associations in the 1860s–70s, the centralising role of the colonial press, the Ilbert Bill controversy (1883), and finally the founding of the Indian National Congress (December 28, 1885) marked progressive stages in the transformation of Indian political consciousness from localised grievance to organised national demand.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** The Moderate phase of Congress (1885–1905) is often dismissed as ineffectual. This is an oversimplification. The Moderates — Dadabhai Naoroji, G.K. Gokhale, Surendranath Banerjee — established the constitutional culture of the Indian national movement: petitioning, statistical evidence, parliamentary argument. They created the organisational framework and the economic critique of colonialism that Extremists and Gandhians later built upon.

**Introduction 3 (Constitutional):** The Moderate demand for "Swaraj within the Empire" and constitutional representation was not weakness — it was a sophisticated strategy that sought to use British liberalism's own promises against British practice. Their demands for expanded legislative councils, civil service reform, and an economic critique of colonialism shaped the constitutional trajectory of Indian politics.

**Introduction 4 (Institutional):** The Indian National Congress (founded 1885) was the first all-India political organisation that created a national identity above regional, religious, and caste divisions — a proto-parliament of Indian opinion that provided the organisational backbone of the freedom struggle for over six decades.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Proto-Nationalist Associations (1850s–1880s)
Before the INC, regional political associations prepared the ground:
- **British India Association (1851, Calcutta):** Zamindari interests initially; later broader.
- **Poona Sarvajanik Sabha (1870):** Founded by Mahadev Govind Ranade; articulated Maratha public opinion; memorials to government; economic critiques.
- **Indian Association (1876, Calcutta):** Founded by **Surendranath Banerjee** — the first organisation with genuinely pan-Indian ambitions; organised all-India conferences on political issues; agitated against age limit reduction in Civil Service examinations (affecting Indian candidates).
- **Madras Mahajan Sabha (1884):** South Indian equivalent; petitioning tradition.
- **Bombay Presidency Association (1885):** Pherozeshah Mehta's organisation — merged into INC.

These organisations shared characteristics: English-educated middle-class leadership, petitioning as primary method, economic critique of colonial policy, and aspiration for greater representation. They were limited by regional scope — the INC solved this.

#### 2. Role of the Press
The nationalist press was the crucial medium for creating a pan-Indian political consciousness:
- *Amrita Bazar Patrika* (Bengal) — nationalist; famously converted entirely to English overnight (1878) to evade the Vernacular Press Act.
- *The Hindu* (founded 1878, Madras) — initially reformist; later nationalist.
- *The Tribune* (1881, Lahore) — Punjab; later associated with Lajpat Rai.
- *Kesari* and *Mahratta* (Tilak, later) — firebrand nationalist.

The **Vernacular Press Act (1878)** — passed under Lord Lytton — imposed censorship on Indian-language newspapers, specifically targeting political comment. Reviled as the "Gagging Act." It was repealed (1882) under Ripon — another Moderate achievement through pressure.

The **Arms Act (1878)** — disarmed Indians while allowing Europeans to carry arms — deeply resented as a symbol of racial discrimination.

#### 3. Ilbert Bill Controversy (1883)
This episode radicalised Indian political opinion more than any other in the early period. Lord Ripon's Viceroy (liberal) proposed the Ilbert Bill — allowing Indian judges to try Europeans in criminal cases in mofussil (rural) courts (they already could in Presidency towns). The white civilian and business community erupted in racial fury — mass meetings, counter-petitions, threats of physical resistance. The bill was emasculated by Ripon under pressure. The "white mutiny" of planters and businessmen revealed that British liberalism had clear racial limits. For educated Indians, the lesson was unmistakable: racial equality was not something the British would voluntarily grant — it had to be demanded. **Surendranath Banerjee** organised Indian response and used the episode to build pan-Indian political consciousness.

#### 4. Foundation of the Indian National Congress (1885)
The INC was founded on December 28, 1885, in Bombay (Gokuldas Tejpal Sanskrit College), with 72 delegates from across India. **W.C. Bonnerji** was the first President.

**A.O. Hume's role:** Allan Octavian Hume — a retired British civil servant — invited political leaders to the founding meeting and initially organised the INC. The "safety valve theory" — attributed to British officials — suggested that Hume founded the INC to provide a "safe" outlet for Indian political discontent, preventing it from becoming revolutionary. Nationalist historians (Bipan Chandra) dispute this — arguing the INC had genuine nationalist content from the start. The truth is probably both: Hume's motivations were mixed; the INC rapidly developed an authentically nationalist character beyond any colonial design.

**Early composition:** Lawyers, journalists, teachers — the English-educated professional class. Significantly, **Badruddin Tyabji** became the first Muslim INC President (1887 Madras session) — reflecting the early secular character of the Congress. The INC was not initially religious-community based.

#### 5. Moderate Phase: Methods and Demands
The Moderate era (1885–1905) was characterised by:

**Methods:** Petitions, memorials, resolutions, speeches in council, delegation to Britain, economic critique through publications. Called "Mendicant Nationalism" by critics; called "constitutional agitation" by proponents.

**Key Demands:**
- Expansion of legislative councils — more Indian elected members.
- Separation of judicial and executive functions.
- Reduction of military expenditure (a drain on Indian resources).
- Simultaneous ICS examinations in India and Britain (age limit discrimination had excluded Indians).
- End of differential tariff policies favouring British goods.
- Economic reforms based on the Drain of Wealth critique.

**Key Personalities:**
- **Dadabhai Naoroji (1825–1917):** Elected to the British Parliament (Finsbury, 1892) as a Liberal MP — the first Indian MP. Used Parliament to raise Indian economic grievances. His Drain theory was the economic foundation of nationalist politics. Three times INC President (1886, 1893, 1906). Called the "Grand Old Man of India."
- **G.K. Gokhale (1866–1915):** Called by Gandhi his "political guru." Moderate; parliamentarian; founded the **Servants of India Society (1905)** — members took an oath to dedicate their lives to India's service. Repeatedly raised economic legislation in the Imperial Legislative Council. Believed in evolutionary reform through constitutional means.
- **Surendranath Banerjee (1848–1925):** "Surrender Not Banerjee"; founded the Indian Association (1876) before the INC; repeatedly agitated against discriminatory policies. Later founder of the *Indian Nation* newspaper.
- **Pherozeshah Mehta (1845–1915):** Bombay; organiser; conservative Moderate.

**Moderate Achievements:**
- The Indian Councils Act (1892) — expanded legislative councils with nominated (not elected) non-official members; gave councils power to discuss budget (though not vote). A small but real advance.
- Created pan-Indian political culture through annual sessions.
- Laid the economic critique of colonialism — essential for later mobilisation.

**Moderate Limitations:**
- Social base: narrow elite; no mass mobilisation.
- Methods: Petitioning was slow and often fruitless.
- No mass agitation strategy.
- Failed to connect with peasants, workers, tribals.

# MICROTHEME 9
## Extremism, Swadeshi Movement, and Revolutionary Nationalism (1905–1920)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The Partition of Bengal on October 16, 1905 — Lord Curzon's administrative decision to divide Bengal along religious lines — was the trigger for a new, militant phase of Indian nationalism. The Swadeshi movement that erupted in response was qualitatively different from Moderate politics: it was a mass movement, it used boycott as a weapon, and it produced the first generation of militant nationalists and revolutionary terrorists.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** The period 1905–1920 saw the simultaneous emergence of three distinct nationalist currents: Extremism (mass agitation, Hindu cultural symbols — Tilak, Lal-Bal-Pal); Revolutionary Terrorism (armed struggle — Aurobindo, Savarkar, Khudiram); and Constitutional Nationalism (Home Rule — Annie Besant, Tilak again). These were not always in conflict; they represented different responses to the same colonial reality.

**Introduction 3 (Ideological):** The Swadeshi movement was simultaneously economic nationalism (boycott of British goods, promotion of indigenous enterprise), cultural nationalism (revival of Hindu religious symbols, Ganapati and Shivaji festivals), and political nationalism (demand for Swaraj). Its multi-dimensional character explains both its power and its limitations.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Partition of Bengal (1905) and Its Significance
Lord Curzon's decision (announced 1905) to partition Bengal into East Bengal and Assam (with Muslim majority) and a rump Bengal (Hindu-majority) was presented as an administrative convenience — Bengal was too large to govern. Nationalists immediately perceived it as a deliberate "divide and rule" strategy: separating the Muslims of East Bengal from the Hindu bhadralok (educated class) of Calcutta, undermining the unified Bengali political culture that had been the centre of the nationalist movement.

The announcement produced an immediate and unprecedented reaction. **October 16, 1905** — the day Partition took effect — was observed as a day of national mourning. Rabindranath Tagore composed *Amar Sonar Bangla* (later the national anthem of Bangladesh); rakhi was tied between Hindus and Muslims as a symbol of unity. The anti-Partition agitation fused emotional, cultural, and political nationalism into a single powerful current.

Partition was **annulled in 1911** (Delhi Durbar) — a major nationalist victory and a demonstration that mass agitation could force the colonial state to reverse a decision.

#### 2. Swadeshi Movement (1905–08)

**Nature:** The Swadeshi movement had several interlocking dimensions:
- **Economic Boycott:** Boycott of British manufactured goods, especially Lancashire cloth. Indians were called on to use indigenous (*swadeshi*) goods. Bonfire of foreign cloth became a symbolic act. Bengal's textile industry and indigenous enterprises benefited.
- **Cultural nationalism:** Ganapati festival (mass religious celebration) and Shivaji festival — both revived by Tilak in Maharashtra in the 1890s — became political tools, allowing mass mobilisation using Hindu religious sentiment. However, this also alienated Muslim participants.
- **Nationalist journalism:** *Kesari* (Tilak's Marathi paper) and *Mahratta* (English) became organs of Extremist nationalism. Tilak's editorials were repeatedly used to prosecute him for sedition.
- **Alternative institutions:** National councils of education, national banks, indigenous industrial companies. These were gestures of civilisational self-confidence.

**Limitations:** The Swadeshi movement was largely an upper-caste, Hindu, urban movement. Muslim participation was limited — partly because of the Hindu cultural symbols used, partly because the Bengal Partition had actually increased Muslim political representation. This communal fracture would widen.

#### 3. The Extremist Ideology: Lal-Bal-Pal Trinity

**Bal Gangadhar Tilak (1856–1920) — "Lokmanya":**
The most important Extremist leader. His philosophy:
- *Swaraj is my birthright and I shall have it* — the most famous nationalist slogan of the era.
- Used religious-cultural festivals (Ganapati, 1893; Shivaji, 1896) as platforms for nationalist mobilisation — reaching beyond the English-educated class.
- Advocated mass agitation, boycott, and non-cooperation as nationalist tools.
- Newspapers: *Kesari* (Marathi) and *Mahratta* (English) — repeatedly tried for sedition (convicted 1897, 1908 — 6 years deportation to Mandalay, Burma).
- Opposed the Moderates' reliance on petitioning. His criticism was sharp: "Mendicancy will never get you rights — demand them."
- Later phase: Founded the **Home Rule League** (April 1916, Pune) — demanding Home Rule (dominion self-government) within the British Empire.

**Lala Lajpat Rai (1865–1928) — "Punjab Kesari," "Lion of Punjab":**
Arya Samaj background; extreme nationalist; deported to Mandalay without trial (1907). His death (caused by injuries from police lathi-charge during protest against Simon Commission, 1928) directly inspired Bhagat Singh to avenge him by assassinating police officer Saunders.

**Bipin Chandra Pal (1858–1932) — "Firebrand of Bengal":**
Advocated "passive resistance" (not mere petitioning), complete self-government, and Hindu nationalist cultural politics. With Tilak and Lajpat Rai, he formed the symbolic trinity of Extremism.

#### 4. Congress Split at Surat (1907)
The Extremist-Moderate conflict came to a head at the Surat Congress (1907). The immediate issue was the election of the next Congress President (Extremists wanted Lajpat Rai; Moderates wanted Rash Behari Ghosh). More deeply, it was about methods — whether Congress should endorse boycott, passive resistance, and mass agitation (Extremist demands). The session broke up in chaos. The Extremists were effectively expelled from Congress. This split weakened the nationalist movement for a decade — and demonstrated that ideological differences required either synthesis or separation.

#### 5. Revolutionary Nationalism
Alongside the Extremists, a more radical current sought armed revolution:

**Bengal:** The **Anushilan Samiti** and **Yugantar Group** organised revolutionary cells. **Aurobindo Ghosh** (later the philosopher of Pondicherry) was connected to these groups. **Khudiram Bose** — 18 years old — threw a bomb at the carriage of Magistrate Kingsford (Muzaffarpur, 1908); the wrong carriage was struck; two British women were killed; Khudiram was hanged. He became the youngest martyr-hero of the revolution.

**Maharashtra:** **V.D. Savarkar's Abhinav Bharat** — a secret revolutionary society. Savarkar was arrested, tried, and sentenced to 50 years in the Andamans (Cellular Jail). His *1857: The First War of Indian Independence* (written in London, 1909) was the first systematic reinterpretation of 1857 as nationalist revolution.

**Punjab:** Revolutionary connections with the Ghadar Party (see below).

**Ghadar Movement:** Founded in San Francisco (1913) by **Lala Har Dayal** — a network of Indian (mainly Sikh) immigrants in North America who organised to return to India and launch an armed revolution during WW1 (when Britain was distracted). The 1915 Ghadar uprising failed due to informers and pre-emptive British arrests, but the movement demonstrated the diasporic dimension of Indian nationalism.

#### 6. Home Rule Leagues (1916)
With Tilak released from prison (1914) and under the altered global context of WW1, constitutional nationalism revived in the form of **Home Rule Leagues:**
- **Tilak's Home Rule League** (April 1916, Pune/Poona): "India for Indians" — demanded Home Rule (autonomy within the Empire).
- **Annie Besant's Home Rule League** (September 1916, Madras/Adyar): More moderate; sought Dominion Status. Besant was the first woman INC President (1917) — elected after she was interned by the British for her Home Rule activities, galvanising sympathy.

#### 7. Lucknow Pact (1916)
The Lucknow Pact between the Indian National Congress and the All India Muslim League (founded 1906 at Dacca) was a significant moment. Congress accepted separate electorates for Muslims (the League's core demand); the League accepted the demand for self-government. This Congress-League cooperation was short-lived — but it established that a united front was possible. The **Morley-Minto Reforms (Indian Councils Act, 1909)** had institutionalised separate electorates — the Lucknow Pact accepted this as a fact, sacrificing the secular ideal for immediate political unity.

#### 8. Jallianwala Bagh Massacre (April 13, 1919)
The **Rowlatt Act (1919)** — allowing detention without trial for political suspects — was the immediate trigger for Gandhi's first pan-India campaign (Rowlatt Satyagraha). On April 13, 1919 — Baisakhi day — a large crowd gathered at Jallianwala Bagh, Amritsar, to protest the arrest of two local leaders. Brigadier-General Reginald Dyer ordered his troops to open fire without warning on the unarmed crowd gathered in an enclosed garden. Official count: 379 killed; civilian estimates: 1,000+; thousands wounded. This massacre destroyed whatever remaining faith educated Indians had in British justice. Rabindranath Tagore renounced his knighthood in protest. It permanently radicalised Indian political opinion and made mass movements inevitable.

# MICROTHEME 10
## Gandhian Era: Philosophy, Methods, and Major Campaigns (1919–1942)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi's arrival on the Indian political scene transformed the freedom struggle from an elite-led constitutional movement into the world's most remarkable mass political phenomenon. Between 1919 and 1942, Gandhi led five major national campaigns, fundamentally altered the social composition of the nationalist movement, and forged a distinctive political philosophy that combined moral principle with practical political strategy.

**Introduction 2 (Philosophical):** Gandhi's political philosophy — grounded in *satyagraha* (truth-force), *ahimsa* (non-violence), and *swaraj* (self-rule) — was not naive idealism. It was a sophisticated political technology, carefully calibrated to exploit the moral and political vulnerabilities of colonial rule. As Gandhi argued in *Hind Swaraj* (1909), the goal was not just political independence but the transformation of India's — and humanity's — civilizational path.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** Gandhi was simultaneously a brilliant mass organiser, a moral philosopher, a shrewd politician, and a social reformer. Understanding him requires holding these dimensions together: the saint who launched mass civil disobedience, the conservative who challenged industrialism, and the Congress politician who yet stood apart from its mainstream. The contradictions in Gandhi are the contradictions of the freedom struggle itself.

**Introduction 4 (Quote-based):** Gandhi's famous injunction — "Be the change you wish to see in the world" — captures the essence of his method: moral transformation as political strategy. For Gandhi, means and ends were inseparable; using violence to achieve a peaceful society was a contradiction in terms.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Gandhi's South African Crucible (1893–1915)
Gandhi spent 21 years in South Africa, where his political philosophy developed through experience. As a young lawyer, he experienced racial discrimination first-hand (thrown off the train at Pietermaritzburg for travelling in a whites-only compartment). He developed and tested satyagraha against discriminatory laws targeting Indians in South Africa — the Asiatic Registration Act (1907), the restriction on Indian immigration, the invalidation of non-Christian marriages. By the time he returned to India in 1915, he had a philosophy, a method, and a reputation.

His foundational text, ***Hind Swaraj*** (1909), written in 10 days on a ship returning from England, sets out his complete world-view: civilizational critique of Western industrial modernity; argument that India's "real" civilization was village-based, spiritual, and non-violent; case for self-rule through self-purification. It remains the most radical critique of modernity produced in the nationalist period.

#### 2. Satyagraha: The Philosophy and Method
**Satyagraha** (Truth-Force or Soul-Force) was Gandhi's foundational concept. It was distinct from:
- Passive resistance (which may be used from weakness)
- Terrorism (which Gandhi explicitly rejected)
- Western civil disobedience (which Gandhi developed but infused with Indian-Hindu spiritual content)

Key principles:
- **Ahimsa (Non-violence):** Not just absence of violence but active love for the opponent.
- **Suffering:** The satyagrahi accepts suffering without retaliation — converting the opponent through moral force.
- **Truth:** The satyagrahi must be absolutely certain of the moral justice of his cause.
- **Self-purification:** Inner discipline (brahmacharya, simplicity, service) as political preparation.
- **Constructive Programme:** Alongside resistance — building alternative social institutions (khadi production, sanitation, Hindu-Muslim unity, untouchability removal) as the positive content of swaraj.

#### 3. Early Indian Campaigns (1917–1919)
Gandhi chose his first three Indian campaigns deliberately — each was local, testable, and demonstrated satyagraha's viability before he scaled up:

**Champaran Satyagraha (1917):** Bihar. Indigo peasants under the *tinkathia* system were forced to grow indigo on 3/20 of their land for European planters at exploitative prices. Gandhi, invited by Raj Kumar Shukla, arrived and began detailed documentary investigation — refusing to leave despite British orders. His moral authority and disciplined organisation produced the Champaran Agrarian Act (1918) abolishing tinkathia. First successful satyagraha in India. **Rajendra Prasad** assisted — beginning his political career.

**Kheda Satyagraha (1918):** Gujarat. Peasants demanded revenue remission after crop failure. **Vallabhbhai Patel** emerged as a key organiser — the beginning of his political career. Campaign ended with informal government concession. Demonstrated satyagraha's applicability to agrarian issues beyond indigo.

**Ahmedabad Mill Strike (1918):** Gandhi mediated between textile mill workers (demanding 35% wage increase) and mill owners. Conducted his **first hunger strike** to pressure the mill owners. Ended with arbitration and 35% increase. Established Gandhi's credibility with industrial labour.

#### 4. Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22)

**Trigger:** Jallianwala Bagh (1919) and the Khilafat issue (pan-Islamic protest against the dismemberment of the Ottoman Caliphate after WW1 — Turkey was being punished by the victorious Allies; Indian Muslims saw this as an attack on Islam).

Gandhi recognised the Khilafat issue as an opportunity to build Hindu-Muslim unity and achieve mass mobilisation simultaneously. The **Khilafat Movement** (led by Mohammad Ali and Shaukat Ali — the Ali brothers) merged with Gandhi's Non-Cooperation campaign.

**Nagpur Congress (December 1920):** The Non-Cooperation resolution was passed. Gandhi promised *Swaraj within one year* — a hostage to fortune.

**Programme:** (a) Surrender of titles and honorary offices; (b) Boycott of government schools; (c) Boycott of law courts; (d) Boycott of legislative councils; (e) Boycott of foreign cloth; (f) If necessary, refusal to pay taxes.

**Significance:** First mass national movement — involved students, lawyers, merchants, peasants. Mobilised hundreds of thousands. Used Hindi for the first time as the medium of nationalist communication (not English). Women participated in large numbers for the first time. The Congress organisational machinery was rebuilt on district-level *pradesh* congress committees — giving it genuine grassroots presence.

**Chauri Chaura Incident (February 5, 1922):** A crowd in Chauri Chaura (Gorakhpur, UP) clashed with police who opened fire; the crowd retaliated, chased 22 policemen into their station, and burnt it with them inside. Gandhi immediately, unilaterally suspended the Non-Cooperation Movement. This was deeply controversial — Nehru, Motilal Nehru, and many others were furious. Gandhi argued that mass disobedience without full non-violent discipline was dangerous and that he would not allow the movement to proceed on false pretences. The withdrawal demonstrated both Gandhi's moral consistency and his political limitations — he had made non-violence a fetish that he would enforce even at the cost of political momentum.

**Swarajist Phase (1922–27):** C.R. Das and Motilal Nehru formed the **Swaraj Party** — entered legislative councils to "wreck them from within." Their council entry was opposed by Gandhi's "no-changers" who wanted to continue constructive programme outside councils. The compromise: both activities were allowed within the Congress. The Swarajists won significant council seats and successfully obstructed government business — demonstrating legislative power.

#### 5. Civil Disobedience Movement (1930–34)

**Context:** The Simon Commission (1927) — appointed to review the GOI Act 1919 — had no Indian members. This produced "Simon Go Back" protests across India. The **Nehru Report (1928)** proposed Dominion Status as the constitutional goal — rejected by Jinnah's 14 Points. At the **Lahore Congress (December 1929)**, under Jawaharlal Nehru's presidency, the resolution was passed for ***Purna Swaraj*** (Complete Independence) rather than Dominion Status. **January 26, 1930** was observed as Independence Day — the date later chosen for the Republic.

**Dandi March / Salt March (March 12 – April 6, 1930):** Gandhi's masterstroke. He chose salt — a commodity used by every Indian, taxed by the colonial state, and carrying profound symbolic resonance (the salt monopoly was a visible symbol of colonial exploitation of the poor). Gandhi marched 240 miles in 24 days from Sabarmati Ashram to Dandi on the Gujarat coast with 78 disciples. On April 6, he picked up a lump of salt — breaking the salt law.

The march was a political genius move: it engaged not just India but world opinion (American, British, European journalists covered it extensively). It gave the entire nation a simple, symbolic act of disobedience — making salt was a crime that every Indian could commit. Civil disobedience spread across the country: salt was made on beaches, foreign cloth picketed, forest laws broken.

**Dharasana Salt Works Raid (May 1930):** Sarojini Naidu led 2,500 volunteers in successive waves to enter the Dharasana salt works. British police beat each wave of volunteers — who did not resist. The American journalist Webb Miller's dispatch, carried worldwide, exposed the brutality of colonial rule to global opinion.

**Gandhi-Irwin Pact (March 5, 1931):** Gandhi agreed to suspend CDM; government released political prisoners, allowed coastal salt-making, agreed to attend the Round Table Conferences. Critics (including Bhagat Singh supporters, left-wing Congress) accused Gandhi of abandoning the revolutionary moment. Gandhi argued the Pact secured a breathing space and brought the Congress to the negotiating table.

**Second Round Table Conference (September–December 1931):** Gandhi attended as the sole representative of Congress. Failed — primarily because of Ramsay MacDonald's Communal Award (separate electorates for depressed classes, i.e., Dalits), which Gandhi strongly opposed. Gandhi returned to India.

**Poona Pact (September 24, 1932):** MacDonald's Communal Award had granted Dalits separate electorates — meaning they would have a separate electorate to elect their own representatives. Gandhi fasted unto death against this — arguing it would permanently separate Dalits from Hindus and perpetuate division. **B.R. Ambedkar** — who had negotiated the Award to protect Dalit political representation — was under immense pressure. The **Poona Pact** emerged: instead of separate electorates, Dalits received a substantially increased number of reserved seats within the joint Hindu electorate, with primaries among Dalits to nominate candidates. Ambedkar later deeply regretted this compromise — arguing Gandhi had used his moral blackmail to deprive Dalits of genuine political independence. The Gandhi-Ambedkar debate on caste and political strategy is one of the most important debates in Indian political thought.

#### 6. Constructive Programme
Gandhi's **constructive programme** was the positive content of his political vision — what India should become, not just what it should overthrow. It included:
- **Khadi and village industries:** Spinning cotton (*charkha*) was simultaneously economic (counter to colonial deindustrialisation), political (mass participation), and symbolic (self-reliance). Gandhi spun daily; the charkha appeared on the Congress flag.
- **Hindu-Muslim unity:** A constant, often unsuccessful, effort.
- **Removal of untouchability:** Gandhi renamed untouchables "Harijans" (children of God). He personally cleaned latrines to challenge the link between caste and sanitation. However, Ambedkar criticised this as paternalistic — treating the symptom without addressing the caste system's roots.
- **Village sanitation, women's uplift, prohibition.**

#### 7. Individual Satyagraha (1940) and Quit India Movement (1942)
*These are treated in fuller detail in Microtheme 16 on the Final Phase.*

**Individual Satyagraha (1940):** With WW2 underway and Congress-British relations at an impasse, Gandhi launched symbolic individual satyagrahas — Vinoba Bhave was the first, followed by Nehru — making anti-war speeches in defiance of wartime censorship. The purpose was to maintain nationalist pressure without giving the colonial government an excuse for mass repression during wartime.

**Quit India Movement (August 8–9, 1942):** Gandhi's most dramatic call — *Do or Die* (*Karo ya Maro*). The Congress passed the Quit India resolution at the Bombay session (Gowalia Tank). Within hours, Gandhi, Nehru, and virtually all Congress leaders were arrested. **Aruna Asaf Ali** hoisted the Congress flag at Gowalia Tank on August 9 — becoming the iconic image of the movement. In the absence of all leaders, the movement became a "leaderless rebellion" — the most spontaneous and widespread uprising since 1857.

#### 8. Gandhi and Women
Gandhi uniquely mobilised women in the nationalist movement — in Swadeshi picketing, salt marches, and Quit India underground networks. **Sarojini Naidu** led the Dharasana raid; **Kasturba Gandhi** was herself arrested repeatedly. However, Gandhi's mobilisation was sometimes paternalistic — protecting women's "honour" and "purity" rather than empowering them as political agents. Scholars like Madhu Kishwar have critiqued this dimension.

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** Gandhi's contribution to India's freedom struggle was irreplaceable — not because he alone drove it, but because he transformed it from an elite project into a civilisational movement, giving it a mass base, a moral philosophy, and a set of non-violent methods that made it politically invincible over time.

# MICROTHEME 11
## Revolutionary Nationalism: Armed Resistance and the Socialist Current (1920–1945)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Ideological):** Revolutionary nationalism occupied the radical flank of India's freedom struggle — rejecting both colonial rule and Gandhi's insistence on non-violence. From the Hindustan Republican Association's Kakori Train Robbery (1925) to the Indian National Army's Imphal campaign (1944), revolutionary nationalists challenged the Gandhian mainstream with a different answer to the same question: how does a colonised people achieve freedom?

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** The revolutionary current had three distinct phases: the anarchist-terrorist phase (1920s Anushilan, Ghadar remnants); the socialist-revolutionary phase of Bhagat Singh (1928–31); and the military-revolutionary phase of Subhas Chandra Bose and the INA (1942–45). Each phase was qualitatively different in ideology and method, but all shared the conviction that non-violence was insufficient.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** Bhagat Singh deserves special recognition as a political thinker, not merely an action hero. His *Why I Am an Atheist* (1930), his *Philosophy of the Bomb*, and his court statements reveal a young man of 23 who had synthesised Marxism, anarchism, and anti-imperialism into an original revolutionary ideology that went beyond both the Gandhian mainstream and the colonial framework.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Hindustan Republican Association (HRA) and Early Revolutionaries

The **HRA** was founded in 1924 by **Sachindra Nath Sanyal** — seeking to organise an armed revolution against British rule through targeted action.

**Kakori Train Robbery (August 9, 1925):** HRA members stopped a train at Kakori (near Lucknow) and seized government treasury funds to finance the revolution. The British launched a massive crackdown. **Ram Prasad Bismil** and **Ashfaqullah Khan** — the most prominent leaders — were hanged (December 19, 1927). Their friendship — one Hindu, one Muslim — became a symbol of revolutionary Hindu-Muslim unity.

Ram Prasad Bismil's *Sarfaroshi ki Tamanna* ("The longing for sacrifice/revolution") became the anthem of the revolutionary movement.

#### 2. HSRA and Bhagat Singh

The HRA was reorganised as the **Hindustan Socialist Republican Association (HSRA)** at Ferozeshah Kotla, Delhi, in 1928 — adding "Socialist" to reflect Bhagat Singh's Marxist influence.

**Bhagat Singh (1907–1931):** The most iconic revolutionary of the 20th century. His trajectory:
- Joined HRA/HSRA in his teens; founded **Naujawan Bharat Sabha** (Youth Organisation of India) to build revolutionary consciousness among youth.
- **Saunders Murder (December 17, 1928):** Lala Lajpat Rai died from injuries caused by police lathi-charges during the Simon Commission protest. Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Chandrashekhar Azad killed police officer Saunders in Lahore — calling it revenge for Lala Lajpat Rai's death.
- **Bomb in Central Legislative Assembly (April 8, 1929):** Bhagat Singh and B.K. Dutt threw smoke bombs in the Assembly — deliberately not killing anyone — to protest the repressive Public Safety Bill and Trade Disputes Bill. Their slogan: *Inquilab Zindabad* (Long Live the Revolution) — coined by Hasrat Mohani but made famous by Bhagat Singh. They surrendered to be arrested, turning their trial into a political platform.
- **Lahore Conspiracy Case:** Tried for the Saunders murder. Bhagat Singh, Rajguru, and Sukhdev were sentenced to death and hanged on **March 23, 1931** — aged 23, 22, and 24 respectively. They were hanged a day early (before March 24) — the British feared public protest.

**Ideological significance:** *Why I Am an Atheist* (written in prison, 1930) argued that religion was a crutch for the weak; that rational thought and socialist revolution were the answer to both colonialism and social oppression. His letters from prison show a person of extraordinary intellectual seriousness — he had read Marx, Lenin, Trotsky, Bakunin, and Irish revolutionary literature extensively.

**Chandrashekhar Azad (1906–1931):** HSRA's military commander. Famously swore he would never be captured alive — shot himself at Alfred Park, Allahabad, on February 27, 1931, when surrounded by police. His self-immolation became legendary.

#### 3. Chittagong Armoury Raid (April 18, 1930)

**Surya Sen / Masterda** — a schoolteacher — led the Indian Republican Army's raid on police armouries in Chittagong (present Bangladesh). The plan was to seize arms, cut telegraph lines, and launch a coordinated uprising. The raid succeeded in seizing the armoury but the arms were not found. Surya Sen and his group then took to the hills for nearly four years — conducting guerrilla warfare against the British. He was finally captured and hanged (January 12, 1934).

**Pritilata Waddedar** — one of Surya Sen's commanders — led the attack on the Pahartali European Club (September 23, 1932) and swallowed poison after being wounded, dying rather than face capture. She is considered the first woman revolutionary martyr of the freedom struggle.

**Kalpana Datta** was another woman revolutionary who participated in the Chittagong group. She was arrested, tried, and sentenced to life imprisonment (later commuted). She later married the communist P.C. Joshi and continued political activity.

#### 4. The Socialist Current within Congress

The **Congress Socialist Party (CSP)** was founded in 1934 by **Jayaprakash Narayan (JP)** and **Acharya Narendra Deva** — within the Congress as a left tendency. The CSP:
- Advocated socialist planning, land redistribution, and nationalisation.
- Connected Congress to kisan and labour movements.
- Provided the organisational energy for the Quit India underground (1942) — JP himself escaped from Hazaribagh Jail and organised guerrilla resistance.

The **Communist Party of India (CPI)** was founded in 1920 at Tashkent by **M.N. Roy** — the first explicitly Marxist-Leninist party. CPI's history in the freedom struggle was complicated: it supported the Quit India Movement in 1942 (British policy toward "People's War" aligned with CPI after Germany attacked the USSR), alienating nationalist opinion. However, the CPI was influential in the Tebhaga movement, the Telangana rebellion, and trade union organisation.

#### 5. Subhas Chandra Bose and the INA

**Subhas Chandra Bose (1897–1945?) — "Netaji"** was arguably the second most important figure in the independence movement after Gandhi. His trajectory was distinct:
- **Congress career:** Twice elected Congress President (1938, 1939). Elected in 1939 against Gandhi's wishes — Gandhi had backed Pattabhi Sitaramayya. Bose won (defeating the Gandhi-backed candidate), demonstrating his popular support, but resigned under pressure when the Congress Working Committee (loyal to Gandhi) refused to cooperate with him.
- **Forward Bloc (1939):** Founded after breaking with Congress — a left-nationalist party.
- **Escape:** Escaped from house arrest in Calcutta (January 1941) via Afghanistan and the Soviet Union to Germany. Organised the Free India Legion from Indian POWs in Europe. Broadcast from Berlin (Azad Hind Radio). Realising Germany could not liberate India, moved to Southeast Asia via submarine (1943).
- **Indian National Army (INA) / Azad Hind Fauj:** Originally organised by **Mohan Singh** from Indian POWs captured by Japan in Malaya and Singapore (1942), then by **Rash Behari Bose** (a senior revolutionary long based in Japan). When Subhas Bose arrived in Singapore (July 1943), he became the supreme commander. He declared the Provisional Government of Free India (Azad Hind, October 21, 1943). The INA established three brigades — Gandhi Brigade, Nehru Brigade, Azad Brigade — and a women's regiment, the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment** under **Captain Lakshmi Sahgal (Dr. Lakshmi)**.
- **INA Campaign (1944):** The INA, fighting alongside Japanese forces, crossed into Indian territory at Manipur — the **Imphal-Kohima campaign**. Indian soldiers on the INA side shouted *Chalo Delhi* (March to Delhi). The campaign failed militarily — Japan's supply lines broke, disease and starvation depleted the INA, and the British-Indian army held Imphal-Kohima. Bose died in a plane crash in Taiwan (August 18, 1945) — though conspiracy theories persist about his death.
- **INA Trials (Red Fort Trials, 1945–46):** The British decided to court-martial INA officers — General Shah Nawaz Khan, Colonel P.K. Sehgal, Colonel G.S. Dhillon. This was a political catastrophe for the British. Congress lawyers (Bhulabhai Desai, Nehru, Tej Bahadur Sapru) defended the accused. Public opinion across India was overwhelmingly sympathetic — INA soldiers were seen as patriots, not traitors. The trials had to be abandoned. Clement Attlee later suggested the INA trials — and the shaken loyalty of the armed forces — were a key factor in British decision to leave India.

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** Revolutionary nationalism complemented, not contradicted, the Gandhian mainstream. # MICROTHEME 12
## The Role of Women in the National Movement

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** Women's participation in India's freedom struggle was transformative — both for the movement and for the women themselves. From **Rani Lakshmibai** at Jhansi (1857) to **Aruna Asaf Ali** raising the Congress flag in Quit India (1942), women defied colonial rule, domestic constraints, and gendered social expectations to become a vital force in the nationalist movement.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** Women's involvement in the freedom struggle was simultaneously liberation and limitation. Gandhi's mass campaigns specifically mobilised women — particularly for picketing and boycott — but often within a framework that valorised their self-sacrifice and purity rather than their political agency. The tension between women as objects of nationalist mobilisation and women as political subjects in their own right is one of the central feminist-historical debates about the freedom movement.

**Introduction 3 (Structural):** The women's question in 19th-20th century India arose in three distinct registers: the social reform debates about sati, widow remarriage, and women's education (Microtheme 6); the political mobilisation of women in the national movement; and the feminist demand for equal rights articulated by the All India Women's Conference (1927) and the Constituent Assembly debates.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Women in the 19th Century Reform Debates
The "women's question" — centred on social reform — was initially *about* women, not *by* them. Male reformers (Rammohun Roy, Vidyasagar, Gokhale) advocated on behalf of women. However, women began to find their voice:
- **Pandita Ramabai** was the exception — an autonomous, extraordinary woman who converted to Christianity (escaping Brahminical constraints), educated herself in Sanskrit, toured Britain and America, and wrote *The High-Caste Hindu Woman* (1887) — a devastating account of the condition of Hindu widows, without male mediation.
- **Rokeya Sakhawat Hossain** — Muslim feminist author — imagined a matriarchal Ladyland in *Sultana's Dream* (1905) and founded schools for Muslim girls.
- The **Hindu Widows' Remarriage Act (1856)** — women's status as widow and widow remarriage — was secured through male reformers but reflected women's lived suffering.

#### 2. Women in the Swadeshi and Extremist Phase
Swadeshi mobilised women primarily through its economic and cultural dimensions:
- Boycott of foreign cloth was a domestic/household act in which women participated actively — choosing not to buy or wear foreign cloth.
- **Sister Nivedita** (Margaret Noble, Irish disciple of Vivekananda) participated in cultural nationalism and connected the women's question to the nation's spiritual regeneration.
- However, the Extremist phase's Hindu cultural nationalism (Ganapati festival, Shivaji cult) was largely masculine in character and did not create a space for women's political leadership.

#### 3. Women in the Gandhian Era — The Transformative Moment
Gandhi's movements specifically mobilised women in unprecedented ways:

**Why Gandhi mobilised women:**
- Moral authority: Women's participation gave the non-violent movement moral credibility — their suffering at the hands of colonial police was powerful political imagery.
- Numbers: Including women doubled the potential participant base.
- Social: Gandhi saw women's participation as a form of *seva* (service) consistent with their social role — simultaneously mobilising and, critics argue, not transcending gender norms.

**Women in Non-Cooperation Movement (1920–22):** Women picketed foreign cloth shops and liquor shops (liquor boycott was a special Gandhi cause). **Kamala Nehru** (Jawaharlal's wife) was active in Allahabad. Many women who had never left domestic spaces entered public political life for the first time.

**Women in Civil Disobedience Movement (1930–34):**
- **Sarojini Naidu** — the "Nightingale of India" — led the **Dharasana Salt Works raid** (May 21, 1930) after Gandhi's arrest. 2,500 volunteers marched in waves; each wave was beaten by police; not one resisted. Webb Miller's dispatch to the United Press moved world opinion. Naidu later became the first woman Governor (UP, 1947–49).
- Women made salt on beaches, spun khadi, led processions, courted arrest.
- **Kamaladevi Chattopadhyay** — socialist feminist — was arrested multiple times.

**Women in Quit India Movement (1942):**
- **Aruna Asaf Ali** hoisted the Congress flag at the Gowalia Tank Maidan, Bombay, on August 9, 1942 — after all male leaders had been arrested. She became the "Grand Old Lady of the Independence Movement."
- **Sucheta Kripalani** organised the underground Congress network.
- **Usha Mehta** ran the secret Congress Radio from Bombay.

#### 4. Women Revolutionaries
Women also participated in revolutionary nationalist violence:
- **Pritilata Waddedar** — led the attack on Pahartali European Club (1932); swallowed poison after being wounded. First woman revolutionary martyr.
- **Kalpana Datta** — participated in Chittagong group; arrested, tried, sentenced.
- **Bina Das** — shot at (but missed) the British Governor of Bengal at a convocation ceremony (1932); sentenced to 9 years.

#### 5. Women in INA
**Captain Lakshmi Sahgal** (née Dr. Lakshmi) — a medical doctor in Singapore — joined the INA and commanded the **Rani of Jhansi Regiment** — the first all-women regiment in Asia. She later returned to India and continued as a doctor serving the poor in Kanpur. She was the Presidential candidate of the Left Front in 2002.

#### 6. All India Women's Conference (1927)
Founded in 1927 — initially focused on women's education but rapidly developed into a political organisation demanding equal rights. By the 1930s, the AIWC was demanding:
- Equal franchise (women got equal voting rights under the 1935 Act in certain provinces; full equality under the Constitution).
- Right to property.
- Marriage reform (opposition to child marriage, polygamy).
- The AIWC was the primary organisational voice for women's rights in pre-independence India and fed directly into the Constituent Assembly debates.

#### 7. Women in the Constituent Assembly
15 women were elected to the Constituent Assembly (1946–49):
- **Sarojini Naidu** — poet, nationalist leader.
- **Hansa Mehta** — wrote the Universal Declaration of Human Rights (she is credited with changing "all men are born free" to "all human beings are born free").
- **Sucheta Kripalani** — nationalist leader; later first woman Chief Minister (UP, 1963).
- **Renuka Ray, Ammu Swaminathan, Dakshayani Velayudhan** (first Dalit woman member) — among the women delegates.

Their presence ensured gender equality was inscribed in the Constitution — Article 14 (equality before law), Article 15 (prohibition of discrimination), Article 16 (equality of opportunity), and Article 21 (right to life and personal liberty).

# MICROTHEME 13
## Constitutional Reforms under Colonial Rule (1858–1947)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The constitutional history of British India from 1858 to 1947 is a story of grudging, incremental concession — each reform coming too little and too late to satisfy nationalist aspirations, but cumulatively constructing the institutional architecture on which independent India's democracy was built.

**Introduction 2 (Constitutional):** The Government of India Act (1935) — though never fully implemented — was the direct template for India's Constitution. Its provincial autonomy provisions, federal structure, and judicial framework were incorporated (with modifications) into the 1950 Constitution. Understanding colonial constitutional history is thus essential for understanding free India's constitutional design.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** The key analytical thread running through colonial constitutional reform was the paradox of representative government under alien rule. Each reform claimed to advance self-government while simultaneously designing mechanisms (communal electorates, nominated members, reserved powers) to maintain ultimate British control and to divide Indian political opinion.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Government of India Act (1858) — Beginning of Crown Rule
The 1857 Revolt ended Company rule. The **Government of India Act (1858)** transferred all powers from the East India Company to the British Crown:
- India now governed by a **Secretary of State for India** (Cabinet minister in London) assisted by a 15-member Council of India.
- The Governor-General was renamed **Viceroy** — representing the Crown directly.
- The Company's Board of Control and Court of Directors were abolished.
- **Queen's Proclamation (November 1, 1858):** Promised equal opportunity in government service regardless of race, religion; respect for treaties with Indian princes; no further annexations; religious tolerance. Largely unimplemented in practice.

#### 2. Indian Councils Acts: 1861, 1892
**Indian Councils Act (1861):** Associated Indians with legislative work for the first time — nominated non-official Indians (including zamindars and princes) to the expanded legislative councils. However, they had no power to initiate legislation or question executive action. Restored legislative powers to Bombay and Madras Presidencies (taken away by Charter Act 1833). Laid the principle of legislative devolution.

**Indian Councils Act (1892):** Enlarged legislative councils; increased non-official members; indirect election introduced (though not explicit). Councils given power to discuss budget and ask supplementary questions. Still no executive accountability.

Nationalist response: Too little, too late. Pherozeshah Mehta: "A reform of no reform."

#### 3. Morley-Minto Reforms / Indian Councils Act (1909)
Named after Secretary of State **Lord Morley** and Viceroy **Lord Minto**. Key provisions:
- **Separate communal electorates for Muslims:** For the first time, Muslim voters elected Muslim representatives separately. This was the most consequential provision — institutionalising communal political identity.
- Enlarged legislative councils at central and provincial levels; elected (non-official) majority in provincial councils.
- First Indian member of the Viceroy's Executive Council: **Satyendra Prasanna Sinha** (Law Member).
- Still no executive accountability to legislature; Viceroy retained all executive power.

**Significance:** The introduction of separate electorates was the most divisive long-term consequence. It gave Indian politics a permanent communal structure — dividing Hindu and Muslim political interests institutionally. Many historians (and Ambedkar in the Poona Pact context) have argued this was the British policy that most effectively advanced "divide and rule." The Lucknow Pact (1916) accepted this structure; the Poona Pact (1932) extended it to Dalits — deepening the segmentation.

#### 4. Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms / Government of India Act (1919)
**Montagu Declaration (August 1917):** Secretary of State Edwin Montagu announced British policy — "increasing association of Indians in every branch of the administration and the gradual development of self-governing institutions with a view to the progressive realisation of responsible government in India." This was the first explicit promise of responsible government — though framed in terms of "gradual" progress.

**GOI Act (1919):** Implemented Montagu-Chelmsford recommendations. Key provisions:
- **Dyarchy** in provinces: Transferred subjects (education, public health, agriculture, local self-government) given to Indian ministers responsible to provincial legislatures. Reserved subjects (law, order, finance, revenue) retained by Governor and his executive council. This split — responsible government for "soft" subjects, colonial control over "hard" subjects — was designed to give Indians experience of administration while retaining British control over the essentials.
- Bicameral central legislature: Central Legislative Assembly (elected majority) and Council of State. But Viceroy retained powers of certification and ordinance.
- **Communal electorates extended** to Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans.
- **Franchise extended** — property-based; very limited (approximately 10% of adults could vote).
- Public Service Commission established.

**Nationalist assessment:** "Unworthy of England to offer; India to accept" (Motilal Nehru Report).

#### 5. Round Table Conferences and Communal Award
**Simon Commission (1927):** Appointed by the Conservative government under Sir John Simon to review the GOI Act 1919. Had no Indian members — provoking "Simon Go Back" protests across India. The Commission's report (1930) became the basis of the 1935 Act.

**Three Round Table Conferences (1930, 1931, 1932):**
- First RTC (November 1930 – January 1931): Congress boycotted (its leaders were in prison). Discussed federal structure, communal representation, safeguards.
- Second RTC (September–December 1931): Gandhi attended as sole Congress representative. Communal question remained unresolved. Conference ended inconclusively.
- Third RTC (November–December 1932): Congress again absent. Finalised most provisions.

**Communal Award (August 1932) / MacDonald Award:** Prime Minister Ramsay MacDonald awarded separate electorates to Muslims, Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans — and **also to "Depressed Classes" (Dalits/untouchables)** — giving them a separate electorate for 10 years (renewable). Gandhi fasted unto death against the Dalit provision (not against Muslim separate electorates — he accepted these as a fait accompli). Ambedkar, under pressure, signed the **Poona Pact** (September 24, 1932) — replacing separate electorates for Dalits with a substantially enlarged number of reserved seats in joint electorates.

#### 6. Government of India Act (1935) — The Most Significant Act
The most comprehensive constitutional document of the colonial period — 321 sections, 10 schedules. Key provisions:

- **Provincial Autonomy:** All provincial subjects transferred to elected Indian ministers responsible to provincial legislatures. The dyarchy of provinces was ended — but Governors retained special powers and the right to take over provincial administration ("Governor's Rule").
- **All-India Federation:** Proposed a federal union of British India provinces and princely states. The princes were to join voluntarily. The Federation was never formed — princes did not join; Congress objected to the terms.
- **Diarchy at Centre:** Reserved subjects (Defence, External Affairs) at the Centre remained with the Viceroy and his Council; transferred subjects with Indian ministers. Since the Federal Assembly was never constituted, the Centre's dyarchy was also never implemented.
- **Federal Court:** Established (precursor to the Supreme Court).
- **Reserve Bank of India:** Established (precursor to RBI).
- **Separate electorates maintained** — extended and elaborated.
- **Franchise expanded** — approximately 35 million voters (still limited).

**Congress response:** Congress denounced it as "a new charter of bondage" — rejected the Federation provisions but contested elections under provincial autonomy provisions (1937).

**1937 Elections and Congress Ministries:** Congress won majorities in 7 of 11 provinces. Congress ministries functioned (1937–39) — valuable administrative experience. They resigned in November 1939 when the Viceroy declared war on behalf of India without consulting Indian representatives.

**Legacy:** The Government of India Act 1935 is the direct ancestor of the Indian Constitution. Its federal structure, list system (Union, Concurrent, State lists), emergency provisions, and judicial framework were substantially incorporated into the 1950 Constitution — modified but fundamentally retained.

#### 7. War-Time Constitutional Proposals (1940–46)

**August Offer (1940):** Viceroy Linlithgow offered expansion of the Viceroy's Executive Council with Indians; post-war constitution-making by Indians. Congress and League both rejected it as insufficient.

**Cripps Mission (March–April 1942):** Stafford Cripps brought proposals: Dominion Status after the war; a constitution-making body; right of provinces/states to opt out of the Union. Congress rejected it — Gandhi famously called it a "post-dated cheque on a failing bank." The Congress objected primarily to the opt-out provision (which could enable Pakistan) and the continued British control of Defence during the war.

**Wavell Plan / Simla Conference (June 1945):** Viceroy Wavell convened conference to form a new Executive Council with equal Hindu-Muslim representation (except for the Viceroy and Commander-in-Chief). Failed because Jinnah insisted that all Muslim seats must be League nominees (not Congress Muslims like Maulana Azad).

**Cabinet Mission Plan (May 1946):** The most important and complex proposal. Three-tier structure: (a) Union of India (Defence, Foreign Affairs, Communications); (b) Groups of provinces (A: Hindu-majority; B and C: Muslim-majority, which could form Pakistan-like units); (c) Individual provinces. The plan was designed to preserve Indian unity while giving Muslims autonomy in Muslim-majority areas. Both Congress and the League initially accepted the plan's outline but both subsequently "interpreted" it to their advantage. The plan ultimately failed — its failure made Partition near-inevitable.

**Attlee's Statement (February 20, 1947):** Prime Minister Attlee announced that Britain would transfer power by June 1948 (later advanced to August 1947). The announcement of a firm deadline ended the ambiguity — creating pressure on all parties to reach agreement quickly, which inadvertently accelerated the rush to Partition.

**Mountbatten Plan / June 3 Plan (1947):** Provided for: Partition of India into India and Pakistan; Partition of Bengal and Punjab; referendums in NWFP and Sylhet (Assam); choice of states to join either Dominion.

**Indian Independence Act (July 18, 1947):** Enacted by the British Parliament. Created two Dominions — India and Pakistan — from August 15, 1947.

# MICROTHEME 14
## Rise of Communalism and the Road to Partition

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Analytical):** Communalism — the political ideology that the interests of religious communities are inherently and permanently opposed — was not a primordial condition of Indian society. It was a modern political construction, shaped by colonial policies (separate electorates, Census categorization), economic competition in a changing colonial economy, and the failures of both the Congress and the Muslim League to build inclusive politics.

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** The Partition of British India on August 14–15, 1947, into India and Pakistan — accompanied by the largest forced migration in history (10–15 million people) and massive communal violence (estimates of 200,000–2,000,000 deaths) — was the most traumatic event in modern South Asian history. Understanding how two countries that had coexisted for millennia came to undergo this catastrophic division requires historical analysis that avoids both simplistic villain-attribution and deterministic inevitability.

**Introduction 3 (Historiographical):** Was Partition inevitable? This is the central historical question. No — it was contingent, shaped by specific political decisions, failures of negotiation, and the acceleration of the transfer of power timeline. Yes — the communal cleavages had become so deep by 1946 that any outcome would have involved significant communal violence and political division. The truth lies in between — and the historical debate illuminates both.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Colonial Construction of Communalism
The British did not invent Hindu-Muslim tension — but they systematised and institutionalised it. Key colonial mechanisms:

**Census:** The decennial census (from 1871) categorised India's population by religion, creating rigidly demarcated religious communities where previously fluid identities had existed. For competitive purposes (government jobs, education, representation), religious identity became the primary political identity.

**Separate electorates (1909 onwards):** The Morley-Minto reforms created Muslim-only electorates. This meant Muslim politicians only needed to appeal to Muslim voters — reinforcing communal appeals as the primary political currency. Once institutionalised, separate electorates became self-reinforcing: communal politics produced communal voters who demanded communal representation.

**Divide and Rule:** The British deliberately played Hindu and Muslim political organisations against each other — each time one demanded constitutional concessions, the British could respond by pointing to the other's opposition. The Communal Award (1932) — giving Dalits separate electorates in addition to Muslims, Sikhs, Christians — was the culmination of the divide-and-rule approach.

#### 2. Muslim Political Consciousness and the League

**Sir Sayyid Ahmed Khan's** late career articulated a proto-two-nation consciousness: after the 1857 revolt, he argued that Hindus and Muslims could not share political institutions because their interests were fundamentally different. He specifically discouraged Muslims from joining the INC (1887). This set the trajectory — though Sayyid Ahmed was a modernist reformer, his political sociology planted seeds that Jinnah later harvested.

**All India Muslim League (1906):** Founded at Dacca — convened by the Nawab of Dacca, Salimullah Khan, and other Muslim notables. A delegation to Viceroy Minto had just secured the promise of Muslim separate electorates (October 1906). The League was initially conservative, loyalist, and elite — representing Muslim landlords and nawabs, not the Muslim masses. It shared this class character with the early Congress.

**Early Congress-League relationship:** Initially competitive but not always adversarial. The **Lucknow Pact (1916)** showed Congress-League cooperation was possible — Congress accepted Muslim separate electorates; League accepted Congress demands for self-government.

**Jinnah's trajectory:** **Mohammad Ali Jinnah (1876–1948)** is the most complex figure in this story. As a young nationalist lawyer, he was called the "Ambassador of Hindu-Muslim Unity" by Sarojini Naidu. He was a member of both the INC and the League, and negotiated the Lucknow Pact. The **Nehru Report (1928)** — which rejected Jinnah's demands (joint electorates with reserved Muslim seats at the Centre; residual powers with provinces) — was the turning point. Jinnah felt betrayed; his **14 Points (1929)** counter-proposed a federal structure with Muslim protections. When Congress rejected these, Jinnah moved decisively toward two-nation theory.

#### 3. Communal Violence and Political Polarisation (1920s–30s)
The 1920s and 1930s saw the first major communal riots of the 20th century:
- **Malabar Rebellion (1921):** Initially peasant + Khilafat agitation; became communal violence between Moplah Muslims and Hindu landlords. Congress-League cooperation in the Khilafat movement ended with mutual recrimination.
- **Calcutta riots (1926), Agra riots (1923), Kohat Massacre (1924, NWFP):** A series of riots deepened communal distrust.
- Hindu Mahasabha and RSS were founded partly in response to perceived Muslim aggression: **Hindu Mahasabha** (1915, V.D. Savarkar's platform from 1937) and **RSS** (1925, Nagpur, founded by **K.B. Hedgewar**) articulated Hindu nationalist ideology. Savarkar's *Hindutva: Who is a Hindu?* (1923) defined Hindu identity in terms of *pitribhumi* (fatherland) and *punyabhumi* (holy land) — explicitly excluding Muslims and Christians.

#### 4. Pakistan Resolution (Lahore, March 23, 1940)
The **Lahore Resolution** of the Muslim League (March 23, 1940) — called the **Pakistan Resolution** though the word "Pakistan" was not used in it — demanded that geographically contiguous Muslim-majority units (NW and NE India) should be constituted into independent states. The term "Pakistan" had been coined by Cambridge student **Chaudhry Rehmat Ali** in 1933 (P = Punjab, A = Afghan/NWFP, K = Kashmir, S = Sindh, TAN = Baluchistan). By 1940, Jinnah had formally adopted the two-nation theory as the League's platform — arguing Hindus and Muslims were two nations and therefore required two homelands.

The Lahore Resolution made Partition a formal political demand — transforming it from an intellectual position to a political programme.

#### 5. Congress Failures and the League's Rise (1937–46)

**1937 Elections:** Congress won large majorities in 7 of 11 provinces; the League performed poorly in Muslim seats (even in Muslim-majority provinces). However, the Congress refused to form coalition governments with the League in UP (where such a coalition was feasible) — instead imposing a condition that the League dissolve its separate identity within Congress. The League, humiliated, concluded that Congress dominance meant Hindu dominance — regardless of Congress's secular claims. This political failure by Congress in 1937 gave the League its best propaganda: proof that Hindus would not share power with Muslims.

**Congress Ministries (1937–39):** While generally competent administrators, Congress ministries were perceived by many Muslims as implementing "Hindi, Hindu, Hindustan" policies — Bande Mataram (objectionable to Muslims as a song of Hindu goddess worship), Hindi imposition, Wardha education scheme (perceived as Hinduising). Many of these perceptions were exaggerated or false, but perception was political reality.

**1945–46 Elections:** After the war, general elections were held. Congress dominated non-Muslim seats; the League won almost all Muslim reserved seats (specifically campaigning for Pakistan). The elections effectively gave the League a democratic mandate for Pakistan from Muslim voters — making Congress's argument that the League did not represent India's Muslims increasingly difficult to sustain.

#### 6. Cabinet Mission Failure — The Last Chance
The **Cabinet Mission Plan (May 1946)** — by Pethick-Lawrence, Stafford Cripps, and A.V. Alexander — was arguably the last serious attempt to keep India united. It proposed a three-tier structure with provinces grouped into Hindu-majority and Muslim-majority groups, with a weak federal Centre. Both Congress and the League initially seemed to accept the plan.

The failure came when: Congress interpreted the plan as allowing provinces to opt out of groups; the League interpreted this as Congress sabotage. Congress President **Jawaharlal Nehru's** press conference (July 10, 1946) — where he suggested that Congress would be free to change the plan once it had power — torpedoed the League's acceptance. Jinnah called for **Direct Action Day**.

#### 7. Direct Action Day and Descent into Violence (August 1946)
**Direct Action Day (August 16, 1946):** Jinnah called for Direct Action — without specifying what it meant. In Calcutta, League-controlled Bengal's Chief Minister Suhrawardy effectively stood the police down. The result was **"The Great Calcutta Killings"** — four days of catastrophic communal violence. Official estimates: ~4,000 killed; unofficial: much higher. Violence then spread — **Noakhali riots (October 1946, anti-Hindu), Bihar riots (November 1946, anti-Muslim), Garhmukhteshwar riots (UP)**. The descent into communal violence removed what little ground for negotiated unity remained.

Gandhi's response — walking alone through riot-torn villages in Noakhali, Bihar — was his most extraordinary moral gesture; it temporarily stopped local violence but could not reverse the political tide.

#### 8. Mountbatten Plan and Partition
Lord **Mountbatten** arrived as the last Viceroy (March 1947) with instructions to effect an orderly transfer of power by June 1948. He determined quickly that:
(a) Unity was impossible — the situation was deteriorating rapidly.
(b) The Partition deadline should be advanced from June 1948 to August 1947.

The **June 3 Plan (Mountbatten Plan)** announced Partition and provided for: Partition of Punjab and Bengal; referendums in NWFP and Sylhet; independence by August 15, 1947. The extremely hurried timeline — giving **Cyril Radcliffe** six weeks to draw the borders of two countries — ensured chaotic boundary demarcation. The **Radcliffe Award** (borders announced August 17, 1947 — two days *after* independence) created maximum confusion and maximum violence in the border regions.

#### 9. Scale and Nature of Partition Violence
The human cost was catastrophic: 10–15 million displaced; 200,000–2,000,000 killed (estimates vary widely); 75,000+ women raped, abducted, or forcibly married; entire communities displaced from ancestral homes. Violence in Punjab was particularly intense — both Hindus and Muslims fled in opposite directions while being killed. **Khan Abdul Ghaffar Khan** ("Frontier Gandhi") — who had led the non-violent Khudai Khidmatgars (Servants of God) in NWFP and had opposed Partition — was devastated: "You have thrown us to the wolves."

Arguments for inevitability: Communal electorates since 1909 had created permanently communal politics; League's 1945–46 electoral mandate was genuine; Congress's 1937 coalition failure was irreversible.
Arguments against: Cabinet Mission offered a workable alternative; Mountbatten's rushed timeline prevented compromise; Nehru's July 10 press conference sabotaged the last chance.

**VALUE ADDITION: Historiography of Partition**

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** Partition was a catastrophe, but free India's response to it — the secular democratic Constitution, the protection of Muslim minority rights, the rejection of Hindu majoritarianism as state ideology — was a conscious repudiation of the two-nation theory. India became the answer to Partition's question: whether a multi-religious South Asian nation was possible.

**Conclusion 2 (Legacy):** The trauma of Partition is not merely history — it lives in the form of unresolved India-Pakistan tensions, the Kashmir question, and the periodic resurgence of communalism in Indian politics. Maulana Azad's warning in *India Wins Freedom* — that Partition would leave India's Muslims in permanent insecurity — has been partially vindicated.

**Conclusion 3 (Historiographical):** The most honest approach to Partition history acknowledges shared responsibility — colonial policies, Congress miscalculations, League intransigence, and the human capacity for violence when identity is politicised — rather than assigning exclusive blame to one actor. This pluralist historical understanding is itself a democratic achievement.

# MICROTHEME 15
## Integration of Princely States and Early Challenges of Independent India

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** On August 15, 1947, India gained independence — but it was not yet a unified country. 562 princely states, covering two-fifths of India's territory and containing one-third of its population, were technically free to choose independence, accession to India, or accession to Pakistan. The integration of these states within the first year of independence — through a combination of diplomacy, persuasion, financial inducement, and, where necessary, military force — was one of the greatest feats of statecraft in modern political history.

**Introduction 2 (Constitutional):** The making of the Indian Constitution (1946–49) and its coming into force on January 26, 1950, was simultaneously an act of political imagination and democratic consolidation. The Constitution that Ambedkar chaired — providing for universal adult franchise, fundamental rights, secularism, and an elected federal republic — was the deliberate choice of a civilisation, not merely a technical document.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** The "early challenges of independent India" — integration of states, refugee crisis, food shortage, administrative inheritance, the Kashmir war — tested the new state's cohesion in its first months. That India survived and consolidated democracy while Pakistan (beginning with similar challenges) moved toward military authoritarianism is one of the most important comparative questions in modern political science.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. The Princely State Problem
The **Princely States** were 562 entities — from large territories like Hyderabad (about the size of France) to tiny states of a few square miles — that had been in treaty relationships with the British Crown under the doctrine of "paramountcy." With the transfer of power, paramountcy lapsed — the princes were technically restored to full sovereignty.

**Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel** — the Deputy Prime Minister and Home Minister — was given charge of the **Ministry of States**, ably assisted by the Secretary **V.P. Menon**. Patel's approach was a combination of political genius and firm pressure:
- **Inducement:** Privy purses — guaranteed annual payments to rulers from the Indian government — were negotiated in exchange for accession. This gave rulers a financial stake in integration.
- **Ideological appeal:** Patel argued that the rulers' interests, as Indians, lay with India; that standing apart would leave them isolated.
- **The Instrument of Accession:** A standard document by which rulers ceded three subjects — Defence, External Affairs, and Communications — to the Dominion of India, while retaining internal administration (initially).
- **Pressure:** Where rulers hesitated, Patel was willing to apply political and, ultimately, military pressure.

By the end of October 1947, all but three major states had acceded: **Hyderabad, Junagadh, and Kashmir**.

#### 2. Hyderabad — Operation Polo
**Mir Osman Ali Khan** — the Nizam of Hyderabad, the richest man in the world at the time — claimed independence, citing the Muslim character of his rule and the size of his state. He sought UN membership and British support. He also harboured the **Razakars** — a paramilitary force of the Majlis-e-Ittehadul Muslimeen — which terrorised the Hindu population and attacked the Congress movement.

Patel moved troops (September 1948), calling the action a "Police Action" (**Operation Polo**, September 17–18, 1948). The Nizam's forces collapsed in 4 days. Hyderabad was integrated. Osman Ali Khan signed the Instrument of Accession. The linguistic Telugu-speaking areas later became Andhra Pradesh; the Marathi-speaking Marathwada went to Maharashtra; Kannada-speaking areas to Karnataka.

#### 3. Junagadh — Plebiscite
**Junagadh** was a small state in Kathiawar (Gujarat) with a Hindu majority (80%+) but a Muslim ruler — Nawab Mahabat Khan — who acceded to Pakistan. India argued this violated the principle that accession should reflect the wishes of the population (as was being applied to Muslim-majority Kashmir in India's favour). A plebiscite (referendum) was conducted — the population voted overwhelmingly for India. Junagadh was integrated.

#### 4. Kashmir — The Unresolved Problem
**Maharaja Hari Singh** of Jammu and Kashmir — a Hindu ruler of a Muslim-majority state — vacillated. He wanted independence. In October 1947, tribal militias from the NWFP (backed by Pakistan) invaded Kashmir. Hari Singh desperately requested Indian military assistance. **Mountbatten** and the Indian government agreed to send troops only if the Maharaja signed the **Instrument of Accession** — which he did on **October 26, 1947**. Indian troops arrived in Srinagar by October 27 and drove back the invaders — but could not complete the operation before a ceasefire was agreed (January 1, 1949) at the UN, leaving Pakistan-administered Kashmir ("Azad Kashmir" + Gilgit-Baltistan) in Pakistani hands.

Prime Minister Nehru committed to a plebiscite in Kashmir (under UN Security Council Resolution 47) — a commitment that was never fulfilled, because the conditions required (Pakistani withdrawal of troops from the ceasefire line) were not met. The unresolved Kashmir question remains the most significant inheritance of the 1947 Partition.

**Sheikh Abdullah** — "Sher-e-Kashmir" — led the National Conference, the dominant political force in the Kashmir Valley. He was closely allied with Congress but later fell out with the Indian government (arrested 1953); his complex relationship with India reflected the unresolved nature of Kashmir's integration.

#### 5. Constituent Assembly and the Constitution

**Constituent Assembly:** Convened December 9, 1946, under the Cabinet Mission Plan. Initially included both Congress and League members — but the League boycotted, leaving it a Congress-dominated body. The Assembly's President was **Rajendra Prasad**; the chairman of the Drafting Committee was **B.R. Ambedkar**.

The Constitution was adopted on **November 26, 1949** and came into force on **January 26, 1950** — chosen as Republic Day because January 26, 1930 had been observed as Independence Day during the CDM.

**Key Constitutional Features:**
- **Federal Structure:** Union and State lists; Concurrent list; strong Centre (in crisis periods, the Centre can take over state governance).
- **Fundamental Rights (Part III):** Articles 12–35 — right to equality, freedom, against exploitation, religious freedom, cultural-educational rights, constitutional remedies (Article 32 = "heart and soul of the Constitution," Ambedkar).
- **Directive Principles (Part IV):** Socio-economic rights not justiciable but guiding policy — living wage, equal pay, free education, public health, etc.
- **Secular democracy:** No state religion; equal treatment of all religions.
- **Universal Adult Franchise:** First constitution in the world to immediately grant universal adult suffrage to a country with 176+ million voters, many illiterate.
- **Independent Judiciary:** Supreme Court with power of judicial review.
- **Reservation:** Dalits and Adivasis granted reserved seats in legislature and government service.
- **Emergency Provisions (Article 356):** Centre can take over a state — used and abused subsequently.

Ambedkar in his valedictory speech to the Constituent Assembly: "Political democracy cannot last unless there lies at the base of it social democracy." This warning — that constitutional democracy requires social equality — remains India's central unresolved challenge.

#### 6. Early Challenges of Independent India

**Refugee Crisis:** Partition produced 7–10 million refugees in India (Hindus and Sikhs from Pakistan). The government had to feed, house, and rehabilitate them — a massive administrative challenge managed largely through the Ministry of Refugees under **Mehr Chand Khanna** and later Gopalaswami Ayyangar.

**Economic Inheritance:** India inherited severe food shortage, low per capita income, a deindustrialised artisanal sector, and an economy geared to British interests. The **Planning Commission** (1950) was established to design a developmental framework — leading to the Five-Year Plans (first plan 1951–56).

**Zamindari Abolition:** The new governments of Uttar Pradesh, Bihar, Madhya Pradesh, and other states abolished zamindari through legislation (1950–52) — fulfilling a century-old demand of the peasantry and directly reversing the Permanent Settlement's legacy.

**First General Elections (1951–52):** India held the world's largest democratic election — 176 million voters, 489 Lok Sabha seats, 401 state assembly constituencies. Congress, under Nehru, won a massive majority. The election demonstrated that universal franchise was both organisationally possible and politically meaningful in India's conditions — confounding predictions of failure.

**Linguistic Reorganisation:** The demand for linguistic states — that India's states should be reorganised along language lines — was intense. **Potti Sriramulu** fasted unto death (December 15, 1952) demanding a separate Andhra state for Telugu speakers. After his death, Nehru conceded — **Andhra Pradesh** became the first linguistic state (October 1, 1953). This opened the floodgates — the **States Reorganisation Commission** (1953) recommended reorganisation; the **States Reorganisation Act (1956)** reorganised India's state map along linguistic lines. This was a major act of democratic accommodation, giving Indian federalism a linguistic cultural foundation.

# MICROTHEME 16
## The Quit India Movement and Final Phase of the Freedom Struggle (1939–1947)

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** The period from 1939 to 1947 was the decisive final phase of India's freedom struggle. World War II fundamentally altered the balance of power — Britain emerged from the war exhausted, financially drained, and politically transformed. The Quit India Movement (1942), the INA trials (1945–46), the Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (1946), and the post-war elections (1946) together created the conditions in which the transfer of power became not just desirable but inevitable from the British perspective.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** The Quit India Movement is paradoxically both Gandhi's most radical call and the movement where his control was most limited. The mass arrests of Congress leaders in August 1942 transformed a carefully planned satyagraha into a leaderless popular uprising — the most spontaneous and violent mass movement since 1857. Understanding QIM requires moving beyond Gandhi to examine the autonomous agency of workers, students, peasants, and local communities who kept the rebellion alive.

**Introduction 3 (Structural):** Why did Britain leave India in 1947? Four factors are essential: (a) War exhaustion and economic inability to maintain the Empire; (b) Changed global order — American anti-imperialism (Atlantic Charter, 1941); (c) Domestic British politics — the Labour government (elected July 1945) had ideological discomfort with empire; (d) Indian political and military pressure — Quit India, INA trials, Naval Mutiny.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Congress Ministries and War Outbreak (1937–1939)
Congress ministries had governed 7 provinces from 1937. Their resignation (October–November 1939) following the Viceroy's unilateral declaration of war on India's behalf was a principled constitutional stand: India could not be committed to a war for democracy without being consulted. The Muslim League under Jinnah celebrated the Congress resignations with a "Day of Deliverance" (December 22, 1939) — marking the depth of Congress-League alienation.

#### 2. War-Time Politics (1940–42)
With Congress out of power, the colonial government relied entirely on its own officials. Gandhi launched **Individual Satyagraha** (1940) — Vinoba Bhave (October) and Nehru (November) made anti-war speeches and were arrested. The purpose was symbolic: maintaining nationalist pressure without the mass mobilisation that wartime might justify suppressing.

The fall of Singapore (February 1942) to Japan and Japanese advance toward India created a new urgency. The British sent the **Cripps Mission** (March–April 1942) to negotiate Indian cooperation for the war effort. The Mission offered: Dominion Status after the war; a constitution-making body elected by the provinces; right of any province to opt out of the Union. Congress (Nehru, Azad) rejected it — primarily because of the opt-out clause (which could enable Pakistan) and because Defence remained with the British. Gandhi's famous description — "a post-dated cheque on a crashing bank" — captured Congress's view.

#### 3. Quit India Resolution and Do or Die (August 1942)
Gandhi convened the All-India Congress Committee in Bombay (Gowalia Tank Maidan, August 8, 1942). He moved the **Quit India Resolution** — demanding an immediate end to British rule in India. His call to the nation: ***Do or Die*** (*Karo ya Maro*) — either achieve freedom or die trying.

The resolution was passed unanimously. Gandhi intended to give the British government 24 hours to respond before launching mass civil disobedience.

The British moved faster: in the early hours of **August 9, 1942**, Gandhi, the entire Congress Working Committee, and virtually all provincial Congress leaders were arrested. Gandhi was taken to Aga Khan Palace, Pune.

**Aruna Asaf Ali** had been on the dais at Gowalia Tank on August 8. On August 9, after all the leaders were taken, she hoisted the Congress flag at the Maidan — in defiance of police. She then went underground, organising the resistance for months before surfacing in 1946. Her image that day became one of the iconic images of the freedom movement.

#### 4. Quit India — Leaderless Rebellion
Without national leaders, the movement became spontaneous and local. Across UP, Bihar, Bengal, Orissa, Maharashtra, and Midnapore (Bengal), workers, students, and peasants:
- Cut telegraph lines
- Destroyed railway tracks
- Attacked government buildings
- Established parallel governments

The most remarkable examples:
- **Satara Prati Sarkar (Maharashtra):** Nana Patil and others established a parallel government in Satara district — with its own courts, police, village councils — that functioned for several years.
- **Tamluk Jatiya Sarkar (Midnapore, Bengal):** A parallel government that distributed cyclone relief when the colonial government failed; controlled substantial territory.
- **Ballia (UP):** The entire district administration collapsed; ordinary people governed themselves for days.

The British response was brutal: 100,000+ arrested; 60,000 detained; collective fines on villages; aerial strafing of crowds. The suppression was among the most intense of the colonial period.

**Why QIM failed in the short term:** Leaders were all arrested; no organisational structure; Japanese advance made it easy for the British to label the movement "pro-Japanese" (unfair, but effective propaganda); internal divergence between Gandhi's non-violence and local armed resistance.

**Why QIM succeeded in the long term:** Demonstrated that the colonial government could only rule India by naked force; shook Indian civil service and police loyalty; internationalised the demand for India's freedom; prepared public opinion for independence.

#### 5. INA and Its Trials (1945–46)
*The INA's military history is in Microtheme 11. Here: the political dimension.*

The **Red Fort Trials (October–December 1945):** The British chose the Red Fort (Lal Qila) in Delhi — a potent symbol — to court-martial three senior INA officers: **General Shah Nawaz Khan** (Muslim), **Colonel P.K. Sehgal** (Hindu), and **Colonel G.S. Dhillon** (Sikh). The choice of officers from three communities was deliberate — and the British didn't realise it would be seen as the INA embodying the very communal unity India sought.

The defence was led by **Bhulabhai Desai** (Congress leader), assisted by **Jawaharlal Nehru** (who wore his barrister's gown again for the first time in decades). Public reaction was overwhelming — INA men were heroes, not traitors. The British were forced to release the accused without completing the sentences. The trials backfired catastrophically.

Clement Attlee, in a 1956 interview, listed the INA trials and the resulting "decline of loyalty to British Crown" among Indian soldiers as a major reason for British departure. If the Indian Army could no longer be relied upon, British rule was physically unsustainable.

#### 6. Royal Indian Navy Mutiny (February 1946)
**February 18–23, 1946:** 20,000 sailors on 78 ships of the Royal Indian Navy mutinied, beginning in Bombay. The triggers: racial discrimination (British officers called Indian sailors "niggers" and "black bastards"), poor food, delayed demobilization after the war. The mutiny quickly gained political dimensions — sailors hoisted the tricolour alongside the naval ensign, and demanded Indian independence.

Ships in Karachi, Calcutta, Madras, and Vizag joined. On land, Bombay workers organised sympathy strikes. The mutiny was settled by the intervention of Patel and Nehru (who urged the sailors to return to duty, promising investigation of grievances) — but the political impact was massive. It demonstrated that the Indian armed forces — the last instrument of British power — were unreliable. The "Royal" Indian Navy became the Indian Navy on independence.

General Auchinleck's report to London: "Indian Army is no longer to be relied upon for internal security." This message was perhaps the single most decisive factor in accelerating British departure.

#### 7. 1946 Elections, Cabinet Mission, and Direct Action
*These are covered in Microtheme 14. Key connection points:*
- 1946 elections gave Congress a mandate for India and the League a mandate for Pakistan.
- The Cabinet Mission's failure made Partition practically unavoidable.
- Direct Action Day (August 16, 1946) and subsequent riots showed that every month's delay increased the cost in lives.

#### 8. Why Britain Left — The Attlee Factor
Multiple factors converged to make British departure certain by 1947:
- **War exhaustion:** Britain was economically devastated; maintaining Indian Empire required resources Britain did not have.
- **Atlantic Charter (1941):** Roosevelt extracted from Churchill a public commitment to self-determination for all peoples — making colonialism ideologically indefensible in the post-war American-led order.
- **Labour government (elected July 1945):** Unlike Conservatives, Labour had long opposed Indian colonialism.
- **Indian political pressure:** Quit India, INA trials, Naval Mutiny created continuous political and military pressure.
- **Attlee's decisive factor (in his own words):** The INA's demonstration that Indian soldiers would fight for India against Britain; the Naval Mutiny; and the perception that holding India by force was both prohibitively expensive and morally untenable.

# MICROTHEME 17
## Cultural Nationalism: Literature, Art, and the Construction of National Identity

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Nations are not only political constructs — they are cultural imaginaries. The construction of Indian national identity was simultaneously a political, cultural, and aesthetic project. Bankim Chandra's *Vande Mataram*, Tagore's *Jana Gana Mana*, Abanindranath's *Bharat Mata* painting, and the Bengal School of Painting were cultural acts that created emotional coordinates for a nation that did not yet exist as a political entity.

**Introduction 2 (Historical):** Cultural nationalism in India emerged in the second half of the 19th century — as the Bengal Renaissance produced literary and artistic works that expressed a new self-awareness, and as the Swadeshi movement turned cultural production into political protest. The printing press, the vernacular newspaper, and the novel were the media through which a pan-Indian national identity was constructed.

**Introduction 3 (Analytical):** Cultural nationalism is both a force of liberation and a potential source of exclusion. Tagore's universalist humanism and Gandhi's village-based spiritualism represent the inclusive vision. Hindu revivalism in cultural nationalism — Ganapati festivals, Shivaji cult, *Vande Mataram* with its invocation of a Hindu goddess — risked the exclusion of Muslims, Christians, and other minorities. This tension defines cultural nationalism's ambiguous legacy.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Bengal Renaissance — The Cultural Foundation
The **Bengal Renaissance** (roughly 1820–1920) was the first major cultural awakening under colonialism. Its output spanned literature, philosophy, social reform, and the arts.

**Bankim Chandra Chattopadhyay (1838–1894):** The first major Bengali novelist and the source of the most contested piece of cultural nationalism — *Vande Mataram*. His novel *Anandamath* (1882) — set in the Sanyasi and Fakir rebellions of the 18th century — contained the song *Vande Mataram* (I bow to the Mother), personifying India as a mother goddess. The song became the anthem of the Swadeshi movement. However, its religious imagery (invoking Durga, Kali) has remained contested — the Muslim League objected to it on religious grounds; the Constituent Assembly adopted only the first two stanzas as the national song (not the full song, which contained explicitly Hindu religious content).

**Rabindranath Tagore (1861–1941):** The most significant cultural figure of modern India. His contributions:
- *Jana Gana Mana* (composed December 1911) — became India's national anthem. (Note: The song was composed for the 1911 Delhi Durbar; some controversy about whether it was composed for George V; Tagore denied this.)
- *Gitanjali* (Song Offerings, 1912) — translated into English by Tagore himself; he was awarded the Nobel Prize for Literature (1913) — the first Asian Nobel laureate.
- After **Jallianwala Bagh**, Tagore renounced his knighthood (1919) — converting an honorary title into a political act.
- **Visva-Bharati (1921):** His educational institution at Santiniketan — synthesising Indian and global cultures.
- **Tagore vs. Gandhi on Nationalism:** Tagore was wary of Gandhi's swadeshi economics (burning foreign cloth was, to Tagore, cultural vandalism — the world's best was to be welcomed, not rejected). His essay "The Cult of the Charkha" critiqued the spinning wheel as a symbol that reduced India to its past. Gandhi's nationalism was rooted; Tagore's was cosmopolitan. Both were sincere; their difference is one of the great intellectual debates of the nationalist era.

#### 2. Bengal School of Painting
The **Bengal School of Art** was a deliberate cultural-political movement — a rejection of the British academic realist style (taught at Calcutta's School of Art) in favour of a "national" Indian style.

**E.B. Havell** — Principal of the Calcutta School of Art — was an Englishman who paradoxically championed Indian art. He removed the European plaster casts from the school and replaced them with Mughal paintings. He argued that the Indian tradition — Ajanta frescoes, Mughal miniatures — was superior to the colonial imitation of European realism.

**Abanindranath Tagore (1871–1951)** — Rabindranath's nephew — was the founding figure of the Bengal School. His style synthesised Indian miniature traditions (Mughal, Rajput) with Japanese Wash technique — producing a distinctly "national" style. His most iconic work: *Bharat Mata* (Mother India, 1905) — a woman in saffron robes holding the symbols of India's civilization (books, sheaves of grain, a garland, a white cloth). This image of India as a divine mother became the visual language of cultural nationalism.

**Nandalal Bose (1882–1966):** The greatest exponent of the Bengal School's mature phase. He painted the illustrations for the Constituent Assembly's handwritten Constitution — the final act of cultural nationalism becoming constitutional culture.

**Sister Nivedita (1867–1911):** Irish disciple of Vivekananda, born Margaret Noble. Her intellectual output connected cultural nationalism to Swami Vivekananda's Vedanta — she edited nationalist journals, encouraged the Bengal School, and articulated the connection between Hindu cultural pride and national consciousness. She died in Darjeeling in 1911.

#### 3. The Role of the Vernacular Press
The printing press — introduced into India from the late 18th century — enabled the construction of a national reading public. Print capitalism (in Benedict Anderson's formulation) created "imagined communities" — people who would never meet but who shared a reading experience and therefore a common identity.

Key nationalist publications:
- *Amrita Bazar Patrika* (1868, Bengal) — became aggressively nationalist; escaped the Vernacular Press Act (1878) by converting to English overnight.
- *Kesari* (1881, Tilak, Marathi) and *Mahratta* (English) — Extremist nationalism; Tilak was tried for sedition from these papers.
- *The Hindu* (1878, Madras) — became a major voice of moderate nationalism.
- *The Tribune* (1881, Lahore) — Punjab's nationalist voice.
- *Yugantar* and *Sandhya* (revolutionary Bengali press).
- *Al-Hilal* (1912, Maulana Azad's Urdu paper) — Islamic modernist nationalism.
- *Comrade* (1911, Mohammad Ali) — Muslim liberal nationalism.
- *Neel Darpan* (Dinabandhu Mitra, 1858–59) — play that exposed indigo exploitation.

The colonial state's response — **Vernacular Press Act (1878), Sedition Act (IPC 124A), Defence of India Rules** — was itself a testimony to the press's political power.

#### 4. National Symbols — Flag and Anthem
The **National Flag** evolved through the nationalist movement:
- 1906: First tricolour (green, yellow, red) hoisted in Calcutta during Swadeshi.
- 1921: Gandhi proposed a flag with the spinning wheel (*charkha*) — a symbol of self-reliance.
- 1931: The Indian National Congress officially adopted a tricolour flag (saffron, white, green with the charkha).
- 1947: The Constituent Assembly adopted the tricolour with the **Ashoka Chakra** replacing the charkha — symbolising the dharmic foundations of the new nation rather than Gandhian economics specifically.

**National Anthem:** Tagore's *Jana Gana Mana* was adopted on January 24, 1950 — two days before the Republic.
**National Song:** The first two stanzas of Bankim's *Vande Mataram* were adopted as the national song.

#### 5. Cultural Nationalism and Its Limits
Cultural nationalism in India was predominantly upper-caste, Hindu in imagery, and Bengali/Maharashtrian in origin. Its limitations:
- **Muslim exclusion:** *Vande Mataram*, Ganapati and Shivaji festivals, images of Bharat Mata — all carried Hindu religious connotations that marginalised Muslims.
- **Dalit exclusion:** The cultural nationalism of Tagore, Bankim, and Tilak spoke little to Dalit lives. It was precisely this exclusion that drove Ambedkar to assert that Dalits were not cultural participants in the Hindu nation.
- **Linguistic exclusion:** Bengal School, Hindi nationalism, and Tamil cultural nationalism sometimes competed rather than collaborated — the national cultural identity was always contested.

Tagore himself was aware of these limits — his critique of narrow nationalism (*The Religion of Man*, various essays) argued for a nationalism that transcended religious and ethnic boundaries.

# MICROTHEME 18
## Labour and Working-Class Movements in Colonial India

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** Industrial labour in colonial India emerged as a distinct social class in the late 19th century — in cotton mills of Bombay and Ahmedabad, jute mills of Calcutta, railways, tea plantations of Assam, coal mines of Bihar and Bengal, and indigo and rubber plantations. This new working class developed its own forms of resistance — from strikes and work-to-rule to the formation of trade unions — that constitute a distinct and often neglected chapter in the history of the freedom struggle.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** The labour movement in colonial India operated at the intersection of three tensions: class struggle (workers vs. employers/colonial capital); national struggle (Indian workers vs. colonial economic structure); and ideological struggle (Gandhi's nationalist-reformist approach vs. the CPI's revolutionary class-struggle orientation). These tensions shaped both the movement's achievements and its limitations.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Industrial Labour under Colonialism
Colonial India's industrial labour was characterised by:
- **Plantation Labour:** Tea gardens (Assam), indigo, rubber — workers brought from tribal and lower-caste communities under indenture-like systems. The **Assam Tea Plantation Workers** lived in near-bondage under the Criminal Law Amendment Act (1908) that made breach of contract by workers a criminal offence. Minimum wages, freedom of movement, and basic rights were denied.
- **Factory Labour:** Bombay cotton mills, Calcutta jute mills — migrant workers (primarily from UP, Bihar for Calcutta; from Konkan, Gujarat for Bombay) in 12–16 hour shifts, no safety regulations, child labour.
- **Railway Workers:** Employed by Company-owned railways; stratified by race (European, Anglo-Indian, Indian) with wage differentials based on race.
- **Mine Workers:** Coal mines of Jharia, Dhanbad — dangerous conditions, no safety legislation.

#### 2. Early Labour Organisation
**N.M. Lokhande** — generally identified as the first Indian trade union organiser. In 1890, he organised Bombay mill workers and demanded a weekly rest day, fixed working hours, and a lunch break. He founded the Bombay Mill Hands Association. His demands were modest by later standards but remarkable for the era.

**Bal Gangadhar Tilak** — an early supporter of mill workers' rights; his mass mobilisation approach (through Ganapati festivals, which mill workers attended) gave him a working-class political base in Maharashtra.

#### 3. All India Trade Union Congress (1920)
The **AITUC** was founded in 1920 — the first all-India trade union federation. **Lala Lajpat Rai** was its first President. Its founding reflected the convergence of nationalist and labour politics following the Rowlatt Act agitation and the Russian Revolution (1917), which had inspired working-class organisation globally.

**AITUC's evolution:** By the late 1920s, the communist influence (led by **S.A. Dange**, M.N. Roy's supporters) grew significantly. The AITUC became a site of conflict between Congress-nationalists and communists — leading to a split (1929) and the formation of separate federations. The **Indian National Trade Union Congress (INTUC, 1947)** was the Congress-affiliated federation; AITUC remained communist-dominated.

#### 4. Major Strikes and Labour Events

**Ahmedabad Mill Strike (1918):** Gandhi's first labour involvement in India. He mediated between workers (demanding a 35% wage increase after the plague bonus was withdrawn) and mill owners (including the Sarabhai family). His method was arbitration combined with a hunger strike — *not* class struggle. Workers were offered 27.5%; they accepted with some negotiation. Gandhi's friendship with mill owner Ambalal Sarabhai reflected his approach: capital and labour should cooperate, not conflict. This made Gandhi fundamentally different from socialist labour organisers.

**Bombay General Strike (1919):** Response to the Rowlatt Act — showed that urban workers were politically conscious and willing to use the general strike as a weapon.

**Meerut Conspiracy Case (1929):** The British arrested 33 labour leaders and Communists — including S.A. Dange, Philip Spratt, and Ben Bradley — charging them with a conspiracy to deprive the King of sovereignty over British India. The trial became an international cause *célèbre* — the defence attracted global support, including from Labour MPs and European socialists. The accused used the court as a political platform. Eventually convicted (1933), sentenced to varying terms; appeal reduced sentences. The case made the CPI's leadership a rallying point.

**Sholapur General Strike (1930):** During the Civil Disobedience Movement, textile workers in Sholapur went on a general strike that briefly turned into an autonomous workers' uprising — workers controlled the city for days. It demonstrated the revolutionary potential of organised labour when aligned with the national movement.

#### 5. Labour Legislation in Colonial India
The colonial state's attitude toward labour was paternalistic and designed primarily to maintain industrial productivity:
- **Factory Act (1881):** First factory legislation — limited child labour; no other significant protection.
- **Factory Act (1891):** Extended protections; women's night work restricted.
- **Workmen's Compensation Act (1923):** Employers had to compensate for industrial accidents — a significant advance.
- **Trade Unions Act (1926):** Gave trade unions legal recognition — allowing registration and protection from prosecution for legitimate union activities. A major gain for the labour movement.
- **Trade Disputes Act (1929):** Restricted the right to strike in "public utility services" — an attempt to limit labour's political weapon.
- **Payment of Wages Act (1936):** Required regular payment; restricted deductions.
- **Royal Commission on Labour (Whitley Commission, 1929–31):** Comprehensive survey of labour conditions; its recommendations shaped later legislation.

#### 6. Women in Colonial Labour
Women were a significant part of the industrial workforce:
- **Tea plantation workers:** Women formed a large proportion; subject to the most oppressive conditions.
- **Cotton and jute mills:** Women as low-wage workers, often working the most dangerous processes.
- **Unequal wages:** Women consistently paid less than men for the same work.
Women's labour was simultaneously exploited and invisible — not represented in trade union leadership until much later.

#### 7. Labour and the National Movement
The relationship between organised labour and the INC was complex:
- Gandhi integrated labour into the nationalist movement — Ahmedabad mill workers, the strike during CDM — but insisted on class harmony rather than class struggle.
- Nehru and the Congress Socialist Party sought closer alliance with labour — advocating socialism within Congress.
- The CPI's strategy was aligned with Moscow's line — which sometimes put it at odds with the national movement (most notably in 1942, when the CPI supported the British war effort, opposing Quit India).
- Post-1947, the labour movement was divided — INTUC (Congress-aligned), AITUC (CPI-aligned), HMS (socialist), and later BMS (RSS-aligned).

# MICROTHEME 19
## Post-Independence India: Nation-Building, Democratic Consolidation, and Legacies

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Historical):** Independent India in 1947 faced challenges that would have broken most new states: partition's refugee crisis, an underdeveloped economy, mass illiteracy, food shortage, the integration of 562 princely states, and communal violence. That India not only survived these challenges but consolidated democracy, conducted free elections, achieved significant economic growth (though slowly), and maintained national unity is one of the great political achievements of the 20th century.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** Post-independence India's political history can be divided into broadly five phases: (1) Nehruvian consensus (1947–64) — socialism, secularism, non-alignment; (2) Crisis and consolidation (1964–77) — Indira Gandhi, 1971 war, Emergency; (3) Democratic reassertion (1977–84) — Janata, Congress return; (4) Economic transition (1984–2004) — liberalisation, coalition politics; (5) Consolidation and competition (2004–present) — UPA, NDA eras, digital economy, nationalist resurgence.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. The Nehruvian Era (1947–1964)

**Jawaharlal Nehru** (Prime Minister 1947–64) set the foundational framework of the Indian state. The "Nehruvian consensus" rested on four pillars:

**Democracy:** Universal adult franchise from the first day; competitive elections (first general elections 1951–52); independent judiciary; free press. India proved that democracy was not the exclusive privilege of affluent or literate societies.

**Secularism:** No state religion; equal treatment of all religious communities; personal law pluralism (Muslims retained Muslim Personal Law; Hindus got the Hindu Code Bills reforming marriage, inheritance, adoption — Ambedkar's major legislative achievement as Law Minister, opposed by conservative Hindus and ultimately causing his resignation in 1951).

**Socialism (socialist pattern of society):** Mixed economy — public sector commanding the "commanding heights" (steel, coal, defence, power, banking); Five-Year Plans; planning commission. Nehru admired the Soviet model of rapid industrialisation. The Second Five-Year Plan (1956–61) under **P.C. Mahalanobis** framework focused on heavy industry — producing the steel plants of Bhilai, Durgapur, Rourkela (the "temples of modern India"). However, agriculture was relatively neglected — contributing to the food crises of the 1960s.

**Non-Alignment:** India refused to join either the American or Soviet bloc — maintaining strategic autonomy. Nehru was a founding figure of the Non-Aligned Movement (Bandung Conference, 1955; Belgrade Conference, 1961). Non-alignment was both idealism and pragmatism — India needed American grain and Soviet steel; non-alignment allowed both.

**Achievements of the Nehruvian era:**
- IITs (first founded 1950s — IIT Kharagpur, 1951) and AIIMS — institutions of higher learning.
- Dams as "temples of modern India": Bhakra-Nangal, Hirakud, Nagarjunasagar.
- Scientific institutions: TIFR, BARC, CSIR.
- Land reform and Zamindari abolition — though uneven in implementation.
- Linguistic reorganisation of states (1956).
- Non-Alignment as foreign policy philosophy.

**Failures/Limitations:**
- Sino-Indian War (October 1962): China attacked along the Himalayan frontier; India suffered a devastating military defeat. Exposed the weaknesses of "Hindi-Chini bhai bhai" foreign policy and India's military unpreparedness. "Himalayan Blunder" — Nehru never recovered psychologically; died in May 1964.
- Food crisis: Agricultural neglect led to dependence on American PL-480 grain — "ship to mouth" existence.
- Bureaucratic socialism: "Licence Raj" — the system of industrial licences that created bottlenecks, corruption, and inefficiency.
- Kashmir — unresolved; Indo-Pakistan war (1947–48) followed by another (1965).

#### 2. Indira Gandhi Era (1966–1984) — Power, Crisis, Emergency

**Lal Bahadur Shastri** (PM 1964–66): A brief but significant tenure. Led India through the 1965 Indo-Pakistan war (Pakistan expected an easy victory after India's 1962 Chinese defeat; the war ended in a stalemate that was actually an Indian strategic success). His slogan — *Jai Jawan Jai Kisan* (Hail the Soldier, Hail the Farmer) — reflected his priorities. Died in Tashkent (January 1966) immediately after signing the Tashkent Agreement.

**Indira Gandhi (PM 1966–77, 1980–84):** The most dominant — and most controversial — figure of post-independence India.

*Major achievements:*
- **Bangladesh Liberation War (1971):** India supported the Bengali independence movement against Pakistan. Pakistan's Operation Searchlight (genocide) drove 10 million refugees into India. Indira Gandhi's diplomacy and military planning (General Sam Manekshaw) produced a decisive military victory — Pakistan surrendered on December 16, 1971 (now Vijay Diwas). Bangladesh was born. The Simla Agreement (1972) with Bhutto established the Line of Control in Kashmir. Arguably India's greatest military-diplomatic achievement.
- **Bank Nationalisation (July 1969):** Nationalised 14 major private banks — making credit available for agriculture and small enterprise, and earning her massive popular support.
- **Green Revolution:** Technical agricultural revolution based on high-yield variety seeds, irrigation, and fertilisers — primarily in Punjab and Haryana — turned India from food-deficient to food-sufficient (though with regional imbalances and ecological costs).
- **Pokhran I (May 18, 1974):** India's first nuclear test — "Smiling Buddha" — established India as a nuclear power.

*Major failures:*
- **Emergency (June 25, 1975 – March 21, 1977):** Allahabad High Court invalidated Indira Gandhi's 1971 election (on grounds of electoral malpractice); the JP (Jayaprakash Narayan) movement had mounted massive anti-government protests. Rather than resigning or allowing democratic processes to work, Indira Gandhi proclaimed national Emergency under Article 352, suspended fundamental rights, censored the press, arrested opposition leaders (including JP, Morarji Desai, Advani), and imposed "Twenty Point Programme" of development. Emergency was the greatest crisis of Indian democracy.
- **Operation Blue Star (June 1984):** Military operation to flush out Sikh militant Jarnail Singh Bhindranwale from the Golden Temple, Amritsar. Deep wound on Sikh religious sentiment; led directly to Indira Gandhi's assassination (October 31, 1984) by her Sikh bodyguards; followed by anti-Sikh pogroms in Delhi (November 1984) in which 2,700–3,000 Sikhs were killed.

**JP Movement and the Emergency's Legacy:**
- **Jayaprakash Narayan (JP)** called for "Total Revolution" — beyond economic reform to moral and social transformation. His movement united opposition parties. Emergency's repeal and the 1977 elections demonstrated India's democratic resilience — Indira Gandhi was voted out; the Janata Party came to power (1977–79).
- The Emergency added the 42nd Constitutional Amendment (1976) — adding "socialist" and "secular" to the Preamble. It also added the 44th Amendment (1978, post-Emergency) — restoring fundamental rights.

#### 3. Post-Nehruvian Social Movements

**Mandal Commission and OBC Reservations:**
The Mandal Commission (1980, V.P. Singh government implemented in 1990) recommended 27% reservation for Other Backward Classes (OBCs) in central government jobs — in addition to the existing 22.5% for SCs/STs. The announcement triggered violent anti-reservation protests (upper-caste student self-immolations). But OBC political mobilisation ("Mandal politics") permanently restructured Indian democracy — empowering large social groups previously marginalised, and creating the social basis of regional parties.

**Babri Masjid Demolition (December 6, 1992):** The demolition of the Babri Masjid at Ayodhya by a mob (allegedly organised by RSS-VHP activists), followed by nationwide riots. This was the most significant communal event since Partition. It destroyed the Congress's secular consensus model; accelerated the BJP's rise; and demonstrated the continuing vulnerability of India's democratic-secular compact.

#### 4. Economic Liberalisation (1991)
India's economy hit a balance of payments crisis in 1991 — foreign exchange reserves could cover only 3 weeks of imports. The Congress government under **P.V. Narasimha Rao** (PM) and Finance Minister **Manmohan Singh** introduced economic liberalisation: removed industrial licensing, reduced import tariffs, opened sectors to private and foreign investment. This was a fundamental shift from the Nehruvian socialist model.

Results: GDP growth accelerated; middle class expanded; IT industry emerged (Infosys, Wipro, TCS); India integrated into the global economy. But inequality also widened; agriculture and informal workers benefited less; environmental costs increased.

#### 5. India's Democratic Durability — Why India Survived as a Democracy
A comparative political science question of great importance: why did India (the world's largest democracy from its founding) survive when Pakistan, Bangladesh, Sri Lanka, and other postcolonial states experienced military coups, authoritarian rule, or civil war?

Key factors:
- **Strong constitutional framework:** Independent judiciary, competitive elections, free press.
- **Political culture:** Congress's democratic precedents; Nehru's personal commitment to democracy (including tolerating opposition).
- **Federalism:** Decentralised power; regional parties check central dominance.
- **Social diversity:** No single ethnic, religious, or linguistic group large enough to dominate permanently.
- **Army's constitutional culture:** Indian Army remained non-political; Nehru ensured civilian control from the beginning.
- **Emergency's lesson:** The 1977 election — where the electorate voted out Indira Gandhi decisively — demonstrated that Indian voters understood democracy and used it as protection.

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** Post-independence India's survival and democratic consolidation — despite a fragmented society, mass poverty, external threats, and the Emergency's assault — is one of the 20th century's most remarkable political achievements. # MICROTHEME 20
## Significant Issues: Communalism, Press, Censorship, and Civil Liberties under Colonial Rule

---

### A. COMMON INTRODUCTION BANK

**Introduction 1 (Conceptual):** Beyond discrete events and personalities, the UPSC syllabus mandates engagement with the "issues" that defined colonial India — communalism as a political construction, freedom of the press as a nationalist demand, sedition law as an instrument of colonial repression, and the civil liberties movement as a precursor to constitutional rights. These are thematic issues that cut across the entire modern period.

**Introduction 2 (Analytical):** Communalism — the belief that religious communities have fundamentally opposed interests — was not an ancient feature of Indian society. It was, as historian Bipan Chandra argued, a modern ideology, constructed by specific political actors (colonial state, communal organisations) for specific political purposes. Understanding its construction is essential for resisting its contemporary manifestations.

**Introduction 3 (Constitutional):** The colonial state's censorship regime — Vernacular Press Act, sedition law (IPC 124A), Defence of India Rules — was the direct ancestor of post-independence controversies about free speech and sedition. The Supreme Court's ongoing engagement with IPC 124A (recently revised as BNS 152) is a living constitutional debate rooted in the colonial experience.

---

### B. BODY

#### 1. Colonial Construction of Communalism
**Census and Community:** The colonial Census (from 1871) created fixed, enumerable religious categories — Hindu, Muslim, Sikh, Christian — and attached them to political stakes (representation, employment). This transformed fluid religious identities into rigid political communities.

**Separate electorates (1909):** The political institutionalisation of communal identity — making religion the basis of political representation. Each reform extended the principle — Morley-Minto (1909) to Muslims; Montagu-Chelmsford (1919) to Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians; Communal Award (1932) extended to Dalits.

**Divide and Rule:** British administrators explicitly managed India through communal arithmetic — balancing Hindu and Muslim political claims, exploiting each community's fear of the other to make both dependent on British arbitration. This was not always a centrally directed conspiracy; often it was the cumulative effect of many individual British officers' calculations.

**Important caveat:** Communalism was not exclusively a colonial creation. Pre-colonial Hindu-Muslim tensions existed; the Faraizi Movement, early Aligarh Movement, and Deobandi revivalism all contained elements that could develop in communal directions. The colonial framework amplified and institutionalised tendencies that existed but might not have led to Partition without the specific political architecture of colonial rule.

#### 2. Hindu Nationalism — Origins and Ideology

**Hindu Mahasabha (1915):** Founded as a platform for Hindu communal politics, the Mahasabha became the vehicle for **V.D. Savarkar's** Hindutva ideology from 1937. Savarkar's *Hindutva: Who is a Hindu?* (1923) defined Hindu identity in political-civilizational rather than merely religious terms — a Hindu was one for whom India was both *pitribhumi* (fatherland) and *punyabhumi* (holy land). By this definition, Muslims and Christians (whose holy land was Arabia or Palestine) were not fully Indian. This was the ideological foundation of Hindu nationalism.

**Rashtriya Swayamsevak Sangh / RSS (1925, Nagpur):** Founded by **K.B. Hedgewar** — a Tilakite nationalist turned Hindu communal organiser — the RSS was initially a character-building organisation for Hindus, using paramilitary shakha (branch) discipline. **M.S. Golwalkar** (*We or Our Nationhood Defined*, 1939) articulated the RSS's ideological framework: admired Germany's treatment of Jews as a model for how India should treat its minorities. Though Golwalkar later distanced himself from this extreme formulation, it remained the ideological DNA of the RSS's early phase. The RSS became the organisational backbone of the Hindutva political movement, spawning the BJP (originally Jana Sangh, 1951) and numerous affiliate organisations.

**Gandhi's assassination (January 30, 1948):** **Nathuram Godse** — a former RSS member, then affiliated with the Hindu Mahasabha — assassinated Gandhi because he believed Gandhi had "appeased" Muslims (particularly his fast demanding India pay Pakistan Rs. 55 crore due under the Partition settlement). The assassination traumatised India and, temporarily, discredited Hindu communalism. The RSS was banned (lifted 1949). Gandhi's death ironically secured the secular Congress's political dominance for two decades.

#### 3. Muslim Communalism — A More Complex Picture

**Deoband vs. Aligarh:** Two distinct streams of Islamic thought represented different responses to colonialism. Deoband — conservative Islamic education; eventually produced the Jamiat-ul-Ulama which opposed Partition. Aligarh — modernist; eventually produced the Muslim League's leadership. Both were responses to the same colonial condition, but they drew opposite political conclusions.

**Jamaat-e-Islami (1941, Maududi):** Founded in pre-Partition India by **Maulana Maududi** — an Islamic revivalist who opposed both British colonialism and Indian nationalism (which he saw as Hindu nationalism in secular clothes). Jamaat-e-Islami sought an Islamic state — a third position between Congress nationalism and Muslim League separatism. After Partition, Jamaat went primarily to Pakistan.

**Hasrat Mohani (1878–1951):** A complex figure — a Muslim communist who coined *Inquilab Zindabad* (Long Live the Revolution) and was a radical anti-colonial voice. He opposed Partition; wanted a composite Indian identity. Represents the anti-communal Muslim voice within the freedom movement.

#### 4. The Press — Freedom and Censorship

**Colonial Press Control:**
The colonial state treated a free press as a threat to be managed, not a right to be protected.

- **Vernacular Press Act (1878):** Under Lord Lytton; targeted Indian-language newspapers with seditious content; magistrates could confiscate printing presses. Repealed 1882 under Ripon; re-enacted in modified form 1908.
- **Sedition — IPC Section 124A (1870):** Criminalised "disaffection" toward the government. Used extensively: Tilak was tried under it (1897, 1908); Gandhi was tried under it (1922); Nehru, Lajpat Rai, Annie Besant were all prosecuted under press/sedition provisions.
- **Defence of India Rules (WW1 and WW2):** Wartime emergency laws gave the executive extraordinary powers to censor, arrest without trial, and suppress publications.
- **Rowlatt Act (1919):** Extended wartime emergency powers indefinitely — "Black Act." Gandhi's Rowlatt Satyagraha was triggered by this.
- **Press Act (1910):** Required press deposits (financial bonds that could be confiscated if "seditious" material was published).

**Nationalist Response:**
Nationalists used the press both to communicate and to demonstrate the contradiction between British liberalism's claims and its colonial practice. *Amrita Bazar Patrika's* overnight conversion to English (1878) to evade the Vernacular Press Act was an act of defiant creativity. Tilak's editorial use of the Ganapati and Shivaji festivals — carefully framed to be legally defensible while politically combustible — was a legal art form.

Key press figures beyond Tilak:
- **Maulana Abul Kalam Azad:** *Al-Hilal* (1912) — radical Muslim newspaper advocating Indian nationalism and opposing British imperialism; suppressed under Defence of India Rules (1914).
- **B.G. Horniman:** Editor of *Bombay Chronicle*; deported by the British (1919) for his reporting on Jallianwala Bagh.

**Annie Besant** — editor of *New India* and *Commonweal* — was interned by the British (1917) for her pro-Indian political activism. Her internment became a cause *célèbre* that accelerated the Home Rule movement; she was elected INC President (1917) while interned.

**Gandhi's response to press censorship:** His newspapers — *Young India* (English), *Navajivan* (Gujarati) — were consistently under colonial scrutiny. He used them to articulate the constructive programme and moral philosophy, not just political protest. He was tried in 1922 for articles in *Young India* — in his famous statement to the judge, he accepted full responsibility and invited maximum punishment.

#### 5. Civil Liberties Movement
The colonial state's repressive apparatus — detention without trial, censorship, restrictions on assembly and speech — produced a civil liberties counter-movement:

- **Civil Liberties Union:** Established in the 1930s, affiliated with the Congress Socialist Party; demanded fundamental rights including habeas corpus, freedom of press, and right to assembly.
- **B.R. Ambedkar** on civil liberties: He was simultaneously a critic of the Congress for its majoritarian tendencies and of the colonial state for its racial authoritarianism. His vision of constitutional rights was partly a response to both.
- **Prison writings as political texts:** Nehru's *Glimpses of World History* and *Discovery of India* (written in Ahmednagar Fort prison), Gandhi's writing from prison, Bhagat Singh's *Why I Am an Atheist* (written in death row) — all demonstrate that colonial imprisonment became a site of intellectual production and political meaning-making.

This is a living constitutional debate with direct colonial roots.

### E. COMMON CONCLUSION BANK

**Conclusion 1 (Nation-building):** The colonial state's suppression of press freedom and civil liberties paradoxically strengthened the nationalist movement — every prosecuted journalist, every banned newspaper, every jailed leader became evidence of the moral bankruptcy of colonial rule. The nationalist demand for civil liberties became the demand for constitutional democracy.

**Conclusion 2 (Constitutional):** Articles 19 (freedom of speech and expression), 20 (protection against arbitrary arrest), 21 (right to life and personal liberty), and 22 (protection against detention and arrest) of the Indian Constitution are direct responses to the colonial censorship regime — constitutional guarantees of what the freedom movement fought for and the colonial state denied.

**Conclusion 3 (Contemporary):** The debate about communalism — whether it is a primordial given or a modern political construction — has direct implications for contemporary India. If communalism is constructed, it can also be deconstructed — through inclusive politics, democratic dialogue, and constitutional secularism. This is the political challenge that modern Indian history bequeaths to the present.

# APPENDIX: CROSS-CUTTING FRAMEWORKS

## Framework 1: Subaltern Perspectives across All Microthemes
The mainstream nationalist narrative focuses on elite leaders — Gandhi, Nehru, Bose, Jinnah. Subaltern history (Ranajit Guha, Dipesh Chakrabarty, Partha Chatterjee) recovers the autonomous agency of:
- Peasants (Champaran, Kheda, Tebhaga)
- Tribals (Birsa Munda, Sidhu-Kanhu)
- Workers (Bombay mill strikes, Sholapur)
- Women (Aruna Asaf Ali, Pritilata Waddedar, the unnamed millions)
- Dalits (Ambedkar's constitutional project, Ayyankali's Kerala activism)
These subaltern actors were not merely supporting cast — they had their own motivations, ideologies, and agency.

## Framework 2: Regional Variations
The national movement played out differently in different regions:
- **Bengal:** Cultural nationalism, revolutionary terrorism, Swadeshi
- **Punjab:** Arya Samaj, revolutionary nationalism (Bhagat Singh), Akali movement

- Economic inequality (redistributed but not eliminated)
